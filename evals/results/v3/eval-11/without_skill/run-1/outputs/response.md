# Positioning Statement

Here's a positioning statement for your clinic scheduling software. I'll give you a primary version, then a fill-in-the-blanks template so you can tune it as your strategy sharpens.

## Primary positioning statement

> **For** small and mid-sized medical clinics that lose revenue to no-shows, double-bookings, and front-desk overload, **[Your Product]** is a scheduling platform that **automates appointment booking, reminders, and provider calendars** so staff spend less time on the phone and more time on patients. **Unlike** general-purpose calendar tools and legacy practice-management suites, **[Your Product]** is **purpose-built for clinical workflows** — handling multi-provider availability, patient self-booking, and HIPAA-compliant reminders out of the box.

## The classic template (Geoffrey Moore format)

Use this when you want to stress-test or rewrite the statement. Fill each bracket deliberately.

> **For** [target customer]
> **who** [statement of need or opportunity],
> **[Product name]** is a [product category]
> **that** [key benefit / compelling reason to buy].
> **Unlike** [primary competitive alternative],
> **our product** [statement of primary differentiation].

Worked example with that template:

> **For** clinic managers and front-desk teams
> **who** are buried in phone-based scheduling, no-shows, and calendar chaos,
> **[Your Product]** is a clinic scheduling platform
> **that** automates booking, reminders, and provider availability so the practice runs full and on time.
> **Unlike** generic calendar apps or bloated, expensive practice-management systems,
> **[Your Product]** is built specifically for how clinics actually schedule — multi-provider, multi-location, and compliant by default.

## Why it's built this way

- **Target customer is specific.** "Clinics" is broad — the buyer and the daily user are usually the practice manager and front-desk staff, so the statement speaks to their pain (phones, no-shows, double-bookings), not abstract benefits.
- **Category is clear.** "Scheduling platform / clinic scheduling software" tells people what shelf you sit on, which makes you easier to evaluate and easier to recommend.
- **The benefit is outcome-oriented.** Fewer no-shows, less phone time, fuller schedules — these map to revenue and staff time, which is what clinic owners actually buy.
- **The differentiation is against real alternatives.** Most clinics today either limp along with generic calendars or pay for heavy practice-management suites. Positioning between those two ("purpose-built but not bloated") is a defensible wedge.

## A few questions that would sharpen this

To make this truly yours rather than generic, it'd help to know:

1. **Who exactly do you sell to?** Solo practitioners, multi-provider group practices, specialty clinics (dental, derm, physio, mental health)? Each implies different pain and language.
2. **What's your sharpest differentiator?** Ease of setup, price, patient self-booking, integrations with EHR/EMR systems, reduced no-shows, or something else? Pick the one thing you'd want a customer to remember.
3. **Who's the real competitor in your deals?** A specific incumbent (e.g., a big practice-management suite), spreadsheets/paper, or a horizontal tool like Calendly? Naming the true alternative makes the "unlike" line land harder.
4. **Is there a proof point?** A metric like "cuts no-shows by 30%" or "saves the front desk 10 hours a week" turns positioning into a promise.
