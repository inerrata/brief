# Landing Page Copy Audit: Loopwork

I read your current copy (`landing-page.md`). Below is a focused audit of why it likely isn't converting, then a rewritten version you can drop in. At the end I flag the assumptions I had to make — those are the things to confirm before you ship.

---

## The core problem in one sentence

**The page describes Loopwork in the vaguest possible terms and never tells a specific person what they get, why it's better, or what to do — so visitors can't decide to act.** Every line could belong to any SaaS product on earth. "Powerful platform for modern teams" doesn't help a marketing manager, an engineering lead, or an ops director picture their own problem getting solved.

Conversion comes from *specificity + clarity + a single obvious next step*. Right now the page has none of the three.

---

## What's broken, line by line

### 1. The headline says nothing
> "The Most Powerful Platform for Modern Teams"

- **"Powerful," "platform," "modern teams"** are all filler. They carry zero information. A reader can't tell what Loopwork *does*, who it's for, or what changes for them.
- **"The Most Powerful"** is an unprovable superlative. Unsubstantiated brag-claims actively lower trust because every competitor says the same thing.
- There's no outcome. Great headlines promise a result ("Ship projects 2x faster," "Stop losing work in Slack threads"), not a category ("platform").

**Fix:** Lead with the specific outcome for a specific audience. Name the pain or the payoff.

### 2. The subhead is buzzword soup
> "Loopwork leverages cutting-edge technology to seamlessly revolutionize the way your team collaborates, communicates, and creates."

- **"leverages," "cutting-edge," "seamlessly," "revolutionize"** — four hype words in one sentence. They're the linguistic equivalent of static. Readers skim right past them.
- **"collaborates, communicates, and creates"** tries to be everything, so it's nothing. A subhead should *narrow*, not widen.
- It explains *how you talk about yourself*, not *what the user gets*.

**Fix:** Use the subhead to say concretely what it is and the one job it does best. Plain words convert better than impressive ones.

### 3. Three competing CTAs cancel each other out
> [Get Started] [Book a Demo] [Learn More]

- Three buttons of apparently equal weight = **decision paralysis**. When everything is primary, nothing is. Conversion drops when you ask the visitor to choose their own path before they're convinced.
- **"Learn More"** is a non-commitment that leaks attention. **"Get Started"** and **"Book a Demo"** imply two *different* funnels (self-serve vs. sales-led) — pick the one that matches how you actually sell.

**Fix:** One primary CTA that states what happens next ("Start free," "Get a demo"). At most one quiet secondary link. Make the button text describe the action and remove risk ("free," "no card," "2 min").

### 4. Features are a context-free list
> - Real-time sync engine
> - AI-powered insights
> - Enterprise-grade security

- These are **features, not benefits**. "Real-time sync engine" is a thing; "Everyone sees the latest version — no more 'final_v3_FINAL'" is a reason to care.
- **"AI-powered insights"** is the most overused phrase in software right now and means nothing on its own. Insights into *what*? Leading to *what decision*?
- **"Enterprise-grade security"** with no proof (SOC 2? SSO? specific certs?) reads as boilerplate.

**Fix:** Convert each feature into a *benefit + proof* pattern: what it is -> what it does for you -> evidence.

### 5. The pricing teaser undercuts itself
> "Affordable plans for everyone. [See pricing]"

- **"Affordable... for everyone"** signals "cheap and generic" and waters down value. "For everyone" is a positioning red flag — products that are for everyone are compelling to no one.
- It also hides the price behind a click with no anchor. If you have a free tier or a clear starting price, *say it* — transparency converts.

**Fix:** State the actual entry point ("Free for up to 5 people," "From $X/user/mo") and what it unlocks.

### 6. What's missing entirely (and costing you the most)

The biggest conversion killers here are the things that *aren't on the page*:

- **No audience.** Who is this for? Naming the user ("for product teams," "for agencies") makes the right person feel seen and filters out the wrong ones.
- **No proof / social proof.** No customer logos, numbers, testimonials, or results. Trust is what tips an interested visitor into acting. With zero proof, a skeptical reader bounces.
- **No differentiation.** Nothing says why Loopwork instead of Notion / Asana / Slack / Linear. If the visitor can't tell you apart, they default to the tool they already use.
- **No risk reversal.** No "free trial," "no credit card," "cancel anytime," guarantee, or security reassurance near the CTA. Removing perceived risk is one of the cheapest conversion wins there is.
- **No quantified outcome.** Numbers ("save 6 hours/week," "cut status meetings in half") are far more persuasive than adjectives.

## Priority order (do these first)

1. **Rewrite the headline** to a specific outcome for a specific audience — biggest single lever.
2. **Cut to one primary CTA** with risk-reducing microcopy ("Start free, no card").
3. **Turn features into benefits + proof.**
4. **Add social proof** (logos, a number, one quote) — even one real testimonial moves the needle.
5. **State pricing/free tier openly** instead of "affordable for everyone."
6. **Strip the hype words** everywhere: leverage, cutting-edge, seamless, revolutionize, most powerful.

## Questions for you (answer these and the copy gets sharp)

The rewrite is generic because I had to guess. To make it convert, I need:

1. **Who is the ideal customer?** Team type, size, role of the buyer (e.g., "ops leads at 20-200-person startups").
2. **What's the #1 job people hire Loopwork to do?** The one problem you solve best.
3. **What's the real differentiator** vs. Notion / Asana / Slack / Linear — why you, not them?
4. **Do you sell self-serve, sales-led, or both?** This decides whether the CTA is "Start free" or "Book a demo" (not both with equal weight).
5. **Any proof?** Customer logos, user counts, testimonials, before/after metrics, security certs (SOC 2, SSO).
6. **Real pricing:** Is there a free tier? What's the starting price?
7. **Any quantified outcomes** you can claim truthfully (time saved, faster delivery, fewer meetings)?
