## The 5-second test (it fails)

A landing page has to answer four questions in five seconds: *What is this? Who's it for? Why should I care? What do I do next?* Right now:

- **What is this?** Unclear. "Platform for modern teams" could be Slack, Asana, Notion, a CRM, or a payroll tool.
- **Who's it for?** "Modern teams" = everyone = no one. Nothing tells me this is for *my* situation.
- **Why care?** The page lists three features but never names a single outcome I'd get.
- **What do I do?** Three competing buttons, none clearly primary.

A confused visitor doesn't convert — they leave. Confusion is the #1 conversion killer, and this page creates it in the first sentence.

## Prioritized fixes (highest impact / lowest effort first)

### 1. Rewrite the headline — it's the single biggest lever
"The Most Powerful Platform for Modern Teams" is a vague superlative that says nothing specific. "Most powerful" is unprovable and every competitor claims it; "modern teams" describes no one in particular. **The headline does ~80% of the work on a landing page**, so this is where almost all your gain is.

Replace it with the *outcome* a real customer gets. (Picking the right one requires knowing what Loopwork actually does — see the questions at the end.)

### 2. Kill the buzzword soup in the subhead
> "Loopwork leverages cutting-edge technology to seamlessly revolutionize the way your team collaborates, communicates, and creates."

This is five buzzwords ("leverages," "cutting-edge," "seamlessly," "revolutionize") stacked into one sentence, and it tries to claim *three* things (collaborate, communicate, create) — which means it claims none. **One page, one message.** The subhead should say, in plain words, *how* you deliver the headline's promise and *who* it's for.

### 3. Fix the CTA — you have three competing buttons
`[Get Started]` `[Book a Demo]` `[Learn More]` all sit at the same weight. Competing CTAs split attention and lower conversion. **Pick one primary action**, make it visually dominant, and demote the rest. "Learn More" in particular is a non-action — it signals you don't know what you want the visitor to do.

Also: "Get Started" is generic. Outcome-specific CTAs convert better — "Start free for 14 days" or "Get a 15-min demo" tell the visitor exactly what happens next.

### 4. Turn the feature list into outcomes
> Real-time sync engine · AI-powered insights · Enterprise-grade security

These are features — they answer *"what is it,"* not *"what does it do for me."* Run each through "so what?" until you hit a benefit the buyer cares about:
- **Real-time sync engine** → "Everyone sees the same numbers instantly — no more 'which version is current?'"
- **AI-powered insights** → "Spot the project about to slip a deadline before it slips." (What does the AI actually surface? Name it.)
- **Enterprise-grade security** → "Pass your client's SOC 2 / security review without a remediation project." (Name the real certification.)

"AI-powered" and "enterprise-grade" are themselves filler unless backed by a specific capability.

### 5. Add proof — there is currently none
The page makes claims ("most powerful," "enterprise-grade") with zero evidence. **Every significant claim needs support.** Add, right under the hero: customer logos, a rating, or one hard stat (e.g. "Trusted by 2,000+ teams"). Then a testimonial with a *specific result* further down ("great product!" doesn't count — "cut our status-meeting time in half" does).

### 6. The page is missing its middle entirely
Right now it jumps hero → 3 features → pricing teaser. A converting page also needs:
- **The problem** stated specifically, so the reader feels understood before you pitch.
- **How it works** in 3 steps, to reduce perceived effort.
- **Objection handling / FAQ** — the real reasons people hesitate (migration pain? pricing? team buy-in?).
- A **final CTA** with a reason to act now.

### 7. The pricing teaser is a missed trust moment
"Affordable plans for everyone" is vague and slightly off-putting (cheap ≠ valuable). Either show a real starting price ("From $8/user/mo") or lead with the risk-reversal that actually lowers anxiety: "Free 14-day trial. No credit card."
