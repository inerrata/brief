## Option B — A pricing-page CRO checklist you can self-audit against right now
If it's faster for you to grade your own page, here's the exact rubric I'd use, in priority order. Pricing pages tend to leak conversions in the same predictable places:

**1. 5-second clarity (the #1 killer)**
- Can a first-time visitor tell *what the product does* and *who it's for* without scrolling? Pricing pages are often reached cold from ads/search, so don't assume context.
- Is it obvious **which plan most people should pick**? If every tier looks equally weighted, you've made the visitor do the work — and confused visitors don't buy. Highlight a "Most popular" plan and anchor the eye there.

**2. Value before price**
- Is each tier framed by the **outcome it unlocks**, not just a feature checklist? "Up to 10 projects" is a spec; "Run your whole team's work in one place" is a reason to buy.
- Are feature lists scannable, or a wall of identical-looking checkmarks? Lead each tier with the *one* differentiator vs. the tier below it.

**3. Price framing & anchoring**
- Show **annual savings explicitly** if you bill annually ("$X/mo, billed annually — save 20%"), and default the toggle to whichever you want to push.
- Order tiers so the recommended plan sits next to a higher-priced anchor — context makes a price feel reasonable.
- Kill **surprise costs.** Per-seat math, add-ons, and "contact us for setup fees" discovered late are a top abandonment cause. Be transparent up front.

**4. One primary CTA per tier, specific and visible**
- Each plan should have **one** unmistakable button. Competing CTAs ("Buy" + "Learn more" + "Compare" all equally loud) split attention.
- Make the copy specific: "Start free trial" / "Get started free" beats a generic "Sign up." Tell them what happens next.

**5. Risk reversal (reduces the anxiety that stalls a purchase)**
- Is there a **free trial, money-back guarantee, or "cancel anytime"** stated *right at the button*? "No credit card required" near the CTA measurably lifts trial starts.
- Do you require a card to start a trial? Each extra field/commitment leaks signups — defer what you can.

**6. Trust at the moment of doubt**
- Proof should sit *near the price and the button*, where hesitation peaks: a customer logo strip, a one-line testimonial with a real result, security/compliance badges (SOC 2, GDPR) for B2B.
- A pricing page with zero social proof asks the visitor to trust you on faith at the exact moment they're reaching for a card.

**7. Answer the top objections on the page**
- A short pricing **FAQ** that handles the real hesitations: "Can I change plans later?", "What counts as a [seat/project/credit]?", "What happens when I hit a limit?", "Is there a discount for annual/nonprofits/startups?"
- Every unanswered objection is a tab-close.

**8. Friction-to-value ratio**
- Count the clicks and decisions between "I want this" and "I'm paying." Each step leaks. Can a plan be chosen and started in one click from this page?

**9. Message-match**
- Whatever ad, email, or homepage link sends people here — does the pricing page's headline and offer echo it? A mismatch between the promise that got the click and what they land on spikes bounce.
