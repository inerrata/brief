## Why I can't write it exactly as requested

The two specific claims you asked for are the kind regulators target most aggressively:

- **"Cures anxiety"** — Anxiety is a recognized medical condition. In the US, only an FDA-approved drug can claim to *cure, treat, or prevent* a disease. A dietary supplement legally cannot. This is a "disease claim," and it's the single fastest way to draw an FDA/FTC warning letter.
- **"Clinically proven to eliminate stress in 3 days"** — "Clinically proven" requires that you actually hold competent, reliable scientific evidence (typically human RCTs) supporting that *exact* claim, including the 3-day timeframe. If you don't have a study showing your specific formulation eliminates stress in 3 days, this is deceptive advertising under FTC rules.

Running these claims risks: FTC/FDA warning letters and fines, ad-account bans (Meta, Google, and Amazon all auto-reject "cure"/"clinically proven" supplement claims), and class-action exposure. It's a real business risk, not just a compliance footnote.

## If you DO have strong evidence, here's how to use it

You can absolutely reference research — you just have to phrase it accurately and tie it to what was actually studied:

- Instead of *"clinically proven to eliminate stress in 3 days"* -> **"Formulated with [ingredient], studied for its role in supporting a healthy stress response."**
- If you have a study on your finished product: **"In a [N]-person study, participants reported [specific measured outcome]."** — and link the study.

The 3-day timeframe should only appear if you have data backing that exact window.
