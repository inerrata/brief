## Why "cures anxiety" and "clinically proven to eliminate stress in 3 days" can't run as-is

These two claims would put you in the single most-enforced corner of advertising law:

1. **"Cures anxiety" is a disease/treatment claim.** In the US, only an approved drug can claim to cure, treat, or prevent a disease (and anxiety is one). A supplement that makes a disease claim is, by definition, marketed as an unapproved drug — that's an FDA/FTC violation, not a gray area.
2. **"Clinically proven to eliminate stress in 3 days" needs the clinical trial to exist *before* you run the ad.** "Clinically proven" requires competent, reliable scientific evidence — a real human study supporting that specific claim — on file *first*. "Eliminate" and "in 3 days" make it worse: absolute, time-bound outcome claims are nearly impossible to substantiate and are exactly what regulators pull.

The liability lands on you (the advertiser), not the ad platform — and Meta/Google will reject supplement ads with cure/clinical language anyway, so it won't even clear review. This isn't me being squeamish; it's the fastest way to get fined and shut down.

**The good news: the compliant version almost always sells better,** because specific and believable beats sweeping and dismissed-as-hype.

## What you *can* say (and how)

Supplements are allowed **structure/function claims** — how an ingredient supports the normal functioning of the body — as long as they're truthful, substantiated, and carry the FDA disclaimer. So instead of "cures anxiety," you say "supports a calm, balanced mood." Instead of "eliminates stress," you say "helps your body manage everyday stress."

### If you *do* have a clinical study

If there's a real human trial behind this, you can absolutely reference it — but phrase it to match what the study actually showed (e.g. "In a [X]-week study, participants taking [ingredient] reported lower stress scores"), cite it, and have qualified regulatory counsel review the wording before launch. "Clinically proven to eliminate stress in 3 days" is a bridge too far even with a study; "clinically studied ingredient shown to support stress response" is defensible if the evidence exists.
