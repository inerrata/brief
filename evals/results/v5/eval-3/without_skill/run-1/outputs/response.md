# Legal Disclosure Requirements for Paying Influencers to Promote Your Fitness App

Short answer: if you pay (or otherwise compensate) influencers to promote your app, the law treats those posts as advertising, and the influencer must clearly and conspicuously disclose the relationship. This applies whether you pay cash, give free access, send free product, offer a commission/affiliate cut, or provide any other perk. The legal responsibility falls on **both** the influencer and you (the brand/advertiser).

Below is what you need to know, organized by jurisdiction, with practical steps. This is general information, not legal advice — for a launch of any scale, have a marketing/advertising attorney review your influencer agreements and disclosure policy.

---

## 1. United States — FTC Rules (the main framework)

The Federal Trade Commission enforces truth-in-advertising law under Section 5 of the FTC Act. Its guidance lives in the **Guides Concerning the Use of Endorsements and Testimonials in Advertising** (16 CFR Part 255), substantially updated in **2023**, plus the FTC's plain-language guide **"Disclosures 101 for Social Media Influencers."**

### When disclosure is required
A disclosure is required whenever there is a **"material connection"** between you and the influencer that the audience wouldn't reasonably expect. Material connections include:
- Cash payment or flat fees
- Free or discounted app subscriptions / premium access
- Free products, gear, swag, or services
- Affiliate links, discount codes, or commissions
- Contest entries, gifts, or any other incentive
- A personal or family/employment relationship

If money or anything of value changed hands, assume disclosure is required.

### What a compliant disclosure looks like
The FTC's standard is **"clear and conspicuous."** Practical requirements:
- **Use simple, unambiguous language:** "advertisement," "ad," "sponsored," or "Paid partnership with [Brand]." Plain words like "Thanks to [Brand] for sponsoring this video" also work.
- **Avoid vague or ambiguous tags:** "#sp," "#collab," "#ambassador," "#partner," "thanks [Brand]," or just "#[brandname]" are **not** considered adequate on their own.
- **Place it where people will actually see it:**
  - Don't bury it in a cluster of hashtags or after a "more" cutoff.
  - Put it **above** the "more" fold, near the top of the caption.
  - Don't hide it on a separate "about" page or only in the bio.
- **Match the format to the medium:**
  - **Video (Reels/TikTok/YouTube):** disclose in the video itself — both **spoken aloud and on-screen as text** is best practice. A description-box-only disclosure is not enough.
  - **Images (Instagram/Stories):** put a clear superimposed text disclosure on the image, especially for ephemeral content like Stories where captions may not show.
  - **Live streams:** repeat the disclosure periodically, since viewers join at different times.
  - **Audio/podcasts:** state it audibly.
- **Use the audience's language:** if the post is in Spanish, disclose in Spanish.
- **Platform tools are helpful but not sufficient.** Instagram's "Paid partnership" label or TikTok's disclosure toggle are good, but the FTC has said you can't rely on built-in tools alone to satisfy the law.

### Additional FTC rules that matter for a fitness app
The fitness/health space draws extra scrutiny because of health-related claims:
- **Endorsements must reflect honest, real experience.** An influencer shouldn't claim results they didn't get, or endorse an app they've never actually used.
- **No false or unsubstantiated health/fitness claims.** Claims like "lose 20 lbs in 30 days," "guaranteed results," or specific medical/health outcomes must be truthful and backed by competent, reliable evidence — and **you, the advertiser, are liable for claims influencers make on your behalf**, even if you didn't write them.
- **No fake or incentivized reviews.** The FTC's 2024 **Rule on Unfair or Deceptive Reviews and Testimonials** (16 CFR Part 465) bans buying fake reviews, paying for reviews without disclosure, suppressing negative reviews, and using fake follower/engagement metrics. Civil penalties can run into the tens of thousands of dollars **per violation**.
- **Typical results / disclaimers:** if you show an exceptional transformation, you may need to clarify that results aren't typical.

### Who is liable
- **The influencer** is responsible for disclosing.
- **You (the advertiser/brand) are also responsible.** The FTC holds brands accountable for their influencers' disclosures and claims. You must have a reasonable program to inform influencers of their obligations and to monitor what they post.
- Ad **agencies and influencer networks** can be liable too.
- Penalties can reach up to ~$50,000+ per violation under current civil penalty rules.

## 2. United Kingdom

Two overlapping regimes:
- **ASA / CAP Code (advertising self-regulation):** Sponsored content must be **"obviously identifiable as a marketing communication."** Use clear labels — **"Ad," "Advert," "#Ad."** Place it up front, before the "more" cut-off. The ASA has explicitly said "#sp," "#spon," "#ambassador," "affiliate," or "gifted" alone are **not sufficient**.
- **Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008 (CPRs) / Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024 (DMCC):** Hidden advertising and fake reviews are illegal and now carry significant enforcement powers and fines (the CMA can fine up to 10% of global turnover under the DMCC).
- "**Gifted**" items still require an "Ad" label if you have any editorial control or expectation of a post. Affiliate links count as advertising.

The ASA and CMA publish a joint **"Influencer's Guide"** that is the practical reference.

## 3. European Union

- The **Unfair Commercial Practices Directive (2005/29/EC)** prohibits hidden advertising; failing to identify commercial intent ("surreptitious advertising") is banned.
- The **Digital Services Act (DSA)** requires that ads be clearly identifiable.
- Member states enforce individually (e.g., France's 2023 **influencer law** imposes specific labeling like "publicité"/"advertising" and "collaboration commerciale," with criminal penalties; Germany has detailed court-driven rules).
- Rule of thumb: label paid content clearly in the local language and don't make unsubstantiated health claims.

---

## 4. Canada and Australia (if you market there)

- **Canada:** The **Competition Bureau** and the ad-standards body **Ad Standards** require disclosure of material connections; the influencer's relationship must be clearly stated. The Competition Act prohibits deceptive marketing and (as of 2022–2024 amendments) carries steep penalties.
- **Australia:** The **ACCC** and **AANA Code of Ethics** require ads to be "clearly distinguishable." The TGA imposes strict rules on **therapeutic/health claims**, which can catch fitness/wellness apps that imply health benefits.

---

## 5. App Store Considerations

- **Apple App Store** and **Google Play** both prohibit incentivized, fake, or misleading reviews and ratings. Don't pay influencers (or anyone) to leave 5-star ratings. This is separate from FTC law but can get your app removed.

## Practical Compliance Checklist

1. **Put it in the contract.** Every influencer agreement should require a clear, conspicuous disclosure ("#Ad" / "Paid partnership with [App]") in a specified location and format, and require truthful, substantiated claims.
2. **Give influencers a one-page brief** with approved disclosure wording, required placement, and a list of health claims they may and may not make.
3. **Specify format per platform** (on-screen + spoken for video; superimposed text for Stories/images).
4. **Ban prohibited tags** (#sp, #collab, #ambassador alone) in writing.
5. **Pre-approve or monitor posts.** Keep a record. Have a process to fix or take down non-compliant posts.
6. **No buying ratings/reviews**, no fake testimonials, no fake follower counts.
7. **Vet health claims.** Avoid weight-loss guarantees, medical claims, and "typical results" implications unless substantiated; add "results not typical" where appropriate.
8. **Localize** disclosures into the audience's language for international campaigns.
9. **Keep evidence** of the substantiation for any performance/health claims, since you (the advertiser) are liable for them.
10. **Get an attorney review** of your template agreement and disclosure policy before scaling, especially given the health/fitness angle.

---

## The One-Sentence Version
If you give an influencer anything of value to promote your fitness app, the post must carry a clear, conspicuous, plainly-worded disclosure (e.g., "#Ad" or "Paid partnership with [App]") placed where the audience will actually see it, every claim must be truthful and substantiated, and **you as the brand are legally on the hook alongside the influencer** — so build disclosure and claim-substantiation requirements into your contracts and monitor compliance.
