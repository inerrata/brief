Short version: if you pay (or give free product, commission, or anything of value to) an influencer to promote your fitness app, the law treats that as an endorsement with a **material connection** — and that connection has to be disclosed clearly and conspicuously, on every piece of content. The advertiser (you), not just the creator, is on the hook for getting it right.

This is practical marketing guidance, not legal advice. The rules below are mostly US-centric (FTC). If you're promoting in the UK/EU or other markets, the principles are similar but the specifics differ — and because a fitness app can drift into health/results claims, I'd have a qualified advertising lawyer review your actual brief and templates before you launch.

## 1. The core rule: disclose the material connection

Any time money, free access, free product, affiliate commission, discounts, or even just a long-standing relationship changes hands, that's a "material connection." It must be disclosed so a reasonable viewer understands the post is sponsored.

- **Clear and conspicuous.** The disclosure has to be hard to miss — in the same language as the post, in the post itself (not only in a profile bio, not buried at the end of a hashtag wall, not hidden behind a "more" tap).
- **Up front, not buried.** Put `#ad`, `#sponsored`, or "Paid partnership with [Brand]" at the start of the caption or clearly on-screen — not as the 14th hashtag.
- **Plain words win.** "#ad," "Paid partnership," or "[Brand] is paying me to share this" are safe. Vague tags like "#sp," "#collab," "#ambassador," "thanks [brand]," or "#partner" are widely considered inadequate on their own.

## 2. Match the disclosure to the format

- **Video (Reels, TikTok, YouTube):** say it out loud AND show it on screen. A viewer who has the sound off or skips the description still needs to see it. For longer videos, repeat it so someone who joins mid-stream catches it.
- **Stories / short-form:** put a visible text disclosure on the frame; don't rely only on the platform's "Paid partnership" toggle. Use the platform tool *and* a plain-language label.
- **Live streams:** repeat the disclosure periodically, since viewers come and go.
- **Audio-only (podcasts):** state it verbally near the mention.
- **Affiliate links / promo codes:** disclose that you earn a commission — the code or link by itself is not a disclosure.

Platform "paid partnership" toggles are helpful but **not sufficient on their own**. Use them in addition to a clear written/spoken disclosure.

## 3. The advertiser is liable — so make it contractual

You can be held responsible for a creator's missing or inadequate disclosure. Protect yourself:

- **Put the disclosure requirement in the contract/brief** as a non-negotiable must-have — specify the exact tag/placement (e.g., "'#ad' or 'Paid partnership' must appear at the start of the caption and on-screen for 3+ seconds").
- **Train creators** briefly on what counts. Many genuinely don't know "#sp" isn't enough.
- **Monitor** posts after they go live and require fixes (or withhold payment) when disclosure is missing or buried.
- **Keep records** of your guidance and your monitoring.

## 4. The claims themselves must be honest — extra important for a fitness app

Disclosure is only half of it. The endorsement content also has to be truthful and substantiated:

- **The creator must actually use and genuinely believe in the app.** You can't script them to say they use it or got results they didn't get.
- **Results claims must reflect typical results, not the best case.** "I lost 20 lbs with this app" implies a typical outcome. If that's exceptional, it needs a clear disclaimer — and "results not typical" in tiny text generally does *not* cure a misleading impression.
- **Health/fitness is a regulated, heavily enforced category.** Avoid disease, treatment, cure, or specific medical/weight-loss claims unless you have real substantiation. "Clinically proven" requires actual clinical evidence. Keep claims conservative and back them up *before* they run. This is the single biggest landmine for a fitness brand — get counsel to review your claim set.
- **No fake reviews or incentivized reviews without disclosure.** US law now carries direct penalties for fake/bought reviews.

## 5. Other things that ride along

- **Free product = paid.** Gifting an influencer a free subscription or gear counts as a material connection. Same disclosure rules.
- **Employees and your own team** promoting the app must disclose the employment relationship.
- **Affiliate program?** Every affiliate posting about you needs to disclose the commission relationship too — bake it into your affiliate terms.
- **Minors:** if your app or the creator's audience skews under 13, extra rules apply (COPPA-style data and marketing limits). Be cautious.

## Quick compliance checklist

- [ ] Material connection disclosed on every piece of content (`#ad` / "Paid partnership"), up front and unmissable
- [ ] Disclosure spoken *and* on-screen in video; visible text in Stories
- [ ] Platform "paid partnership" tool used *in addition to* a plain-language label
- [ ] Disclosure requirement written into the contract/brief with exact placement specified
- [ ] Creators briefed; posts monitored after going live; payment tied to compliance
- [ ] Creator actually uses and believes in the app — no scripted false claims
- [ ] Any results/health claims are typical, substantiated before posting, and reviewed by counsel
- [ ] Affiliate/promo-code commission relationships disclosed
- [ ] Records kept of guidance and monitoring
