Expanding skincare from the US into the EU is less a translation project and more a re-build of your claims, your data practices, and your trust signals. Cosmetics is a regulated category, and the EU's rules are stricter and structurally different from the US. Below is what actually changes, ordered by how much it can hurt you if you get it wrong.

*Assuming you mean the EU/EEA broadly (not a single country first) and that you sell direct-to-consumer online; if you're entering one market first or going wholesale/retail, some of this shifts. This is practical marketing guidance, not legal advice — for a regulated category in a new market, have EU regulatory counsel and a Responsible Person review before you launch.*

---

## 1. Your product claims are the biggest change — most US skincare claims won't survive

This is where US brands get burned. The EU regulates cosmetic claims under the Cosmetics Regulation (EC 1223/2009) and the Common Criteria for claims. The bar is "claims must be substantiated, honest, fair, and not misleading" — and enforcement is real.

What typically has to change in your copy:

- **No drug-like / treatment claims.** A cosmetic in the EU cleanses, protects, perfumes, keeps in good condition, or changes appearance. Words like "treats," "cures," "repairs," "heals," "anti-inflammatory," or claims to act on acne, eczema, or rosacea push you into *medicinal product* or *medical device* territory — a different, far heavier regulatory regime. Reframe to appearance/feel: "visibly reduces the look of blemishes," not "treats acne."
- **"Anti-aging," "anti-wrinkle," "boosts collagen," "repairs the skin barrier"** — these need real evidence on file *before* you make them, and the strong physiological versions are often not defensible for a cosmetic. Soften to appearance claims with substantiation.
- **"Clean," "natural," "non-toxic," "chemical-free," "hypoallergenic"** — "free-from" and "clean" claims are specifically restricted under the Common Criteria. "Free from [ingredient]" is generally not allowed if it denigrates a legally permitted, safe ingredient (e.g. "paraben-free," "free from preservatives"). "Chemical-free" is treated as misleading. "Hypoallergenic" requires a very high evidence bar.
- **"Dermatologist tested," "clinically proven," "97% of users saw…"** — all need the actual test/study/panel on file before the claim runs. Keep the receipts; an EU authority can ask.
- **Environmental / "green" claims** are tightening hard. The EU's Green Claims and anti-greenwashing rules require sustainability claims to be specific and substantiated — vague "eco-friendly," "sustainable," "planet-positive" language is a growing enforcement target. "Recyclable" and "biodegradable" need qualification.

**Action:** audit every claim on your site, packaging, and ads against the EU framework, sort into keep / soften / drop, and make sure each surviving claim has evidence on file *before* launch. This is the single highest-leverage thing you can do.

## 2. Regulatory infrastructure that gates whether you can market at all

These aren't marketing tasks, but your launch timeline depends on them, so flag them to whoever owns regulatory:

- **Responsible Person (RP):** every product sold in the EU needs a legal entity established in the EU acting as Responsible Person. No RP, no legal sale.
- **CPNP notification:** each product must be notified in the EU's Cosmetic Products Notification Portal before it goes on the market.
- **PIF + safety assessment:** a Product Information File and an EU safety assessment per product.
- **Ingredients:** the EU bans/restricts substances the US allows. Some of your current formulas or shade ranges may need reformulation, which can change what you're allowed to *say* about them.
- **Labeling:** ingredient lists in INCI, labeling in the official language(s) of each market you sell in, specific symbols (period-after-opening, etc.). Your packaging and any "ingredients" content on-site changes.

## 3. Email, consent and tracking — GDPR flips your defaults

Your US growth playbook probably assumes you can email acquired lists and drop tracking pixels on arrival. In the EU you can't.

- **Email is opt-in, not opt-out.** GDPR/PECR generally require prior, freely-given, specific, informed consent before marketing email. Pre-checked boxes don't count. You can't import a US-style purchased or scraped list and start sending. Build EU consent fresh.
- **Cookie/consent banner before tracking.** No non-essential analytics, ad pixels, or retargeting tags fire before the user consents. This affects your Meta/Google retargeting and your attribution — plan for measurement gaps versus what you're used to in the US.
- **Privacy notice + data rights.** Clear notice of what you collect and why; honor access/deletion requests.
- **Right to withdraw** must be as easy as opting in.

Practical effect on marketing: your paid-social retargeting and lookalike audiences will be thinner and slower to build, and email list growth starts from zero with consented contacts. Lean harder on first-party consented signups (incentivized, disclosed) and on channels that don't depend on cross-site tracking.

## 4. Localization — it's not "translate the site"

The EU is ~24 official languages and very different markets. Don't treat it as one.

- **Pick your beachhead.** Don't localize for all of Europe at once. Prioritize 1–3 markets by real demand and ease (often something like Germany, France, plus an English-comfortable market like the Netherlands or Ireland). Get one right, then expand.
- **Transcreate, don't translate, the high-impact copy.** Taglines, hero headlines, campaign concepts get *recreated* to land natively — a literal translation of a US tagline usually falls flat or reads as foreign. Product descriptions and legal text get translated/localized straight.
- **Native, in-market review before anything ships.** This is the most-skipped, highest-leverage step. A fluent translator isn't enough; you want someone who lives in the culture to catch what reads as off.
- **Formality register is a real decision** — German Sie vs. du, French vous vs. tu. It's a brand-voice choice per language, not a default.
- **Locale mechanics:** prices in EUR (priced *to the market*, not just converted), comma decimal separator (19,90 EUR not $19.90), DD/MM dates, metric units, EU sizing.
- **Local trust signals.** Swap US press logos and US-customer testimonials for local proof where you have it; "As seen in [US magazine]" means little in Munich. Imagery should reflect local audiences.
- **Channel mix varies by market** — Google/Meta dominate most of the EU, but check per market, and note that some channels behave differently under EU privacy rules.

## 5. Search and SEO restart per language

- **Keywords don't translate — re-research them natively.** The literal translation of your best US keyword may have no volume or the wrong intent. Redo keyword research in-language per market.
- **Technical i18n:** hreflang tags and a clear URL structure (ccTLD vs. subdirectory vs. subdomain) so the right market gets the right page, plus localized metadata.

## 6. Consumer-protection and ad rules are stricter than the US

- **Pricing/discounts:** the Omnibus Directive requires that "was/now" and "X% off" reference the *lowest price in the prior 30 days* — you can't anchor against an inflated "original" price the way some US promos do.
- **No dark patterns.** Fake countdown timers that reset, "only 2 left" that isn't true, confirmshaming opt-outs, hard-to-cancel subscriptions — increasingly illegal under EU rules and actively enforced. Real urgency is fine; fabricated urgency isn't.
- **Subscriptions:** cancellation must be at least as easy as signup. If you run a replenishment/subscribe-and-save program, the cancel flow has to be clean.
- **Influencer disclosure:** material connections (paid, gifted product, affiliate) must be disclosed clearly and up front — "#ad" / paid-partnership label, not buried. The brand is liable, not just the creator. EU markets enforce this actively, and gifting PR product to beauty creators counts as a material connection.

## What I'd do, in order

1. **Claims audit** — run every on-site, on-pack, and ad claim against the EU framework; sort keep/soften/drop; confirm evidence on file for survivors. (Marketing can start this now.)
2. **Regulatory track in parallel** — RP, CPNP, PIF, formula/label review. Marketing launch date depends on it.
3. **Rebuild consent + tracking** for GDPR — fresh opt-in email capture, consent banner, privacy notice.
4. **Pick 1–3 beachhead markets**, then localize/transcreate for those rather than all of Europe.
5. **Reset SEO** with native keyword research and hreflang.
6. **Scrub promos and flows** for Omnibus pricing, dark patterns, and clean cancellation.
7. **Native in-market review** of everything customer-facing before launch.
