## The hard part: don't blast the purchased list

Emailing a bought list of 50,000 cold addresses isn't just a low-converting tactic — in most places it's against the law and against every major email provider's rules. The short version:

- **US (CAN-SPAM):** sending to harvested or purchased address lists is specifically prohibited. Fines run up to ~$53k *per email*.
- **EU / UK (GDPR / PECR):** marketing email requires prior opt-in consent from each person. A list you bought has no consent you can prove, so every send is a violation.
- **Canada (CASL):** among the strictest — express consent required, steep penalties.
- **Your sending reputation:** purchased lists are full of spam traps and dead addresses. One blast typically triggers spam complaints, gets your domain blacklisted, and can permanently wreck your deliverability — so even your *legitimate* future emails land in spam. Tools like Mailchimp, SendGrid, and HubSpot will suspend your account for uploading a bought list.

So the bad news is I can't write you a "blast to all 50,000" email — there's no version of that which is both legal and effective. The money you spent on the list is unfortunately a sunk cost; please don't send to it.

The good news: the actual goal underneath this — *get in front of cold prospects and turn them into customers* — is very doable. Here are the two clean paths, with ready-to-use copy for each.

---

## Path 1 (recommended): turn cold outreach into an opt-in funnel

Instead of emailing strangers, run a paid ad or landing page that gets the *same kind of people* to raise their hand. Then you email people who asked to hear from you — fully compliant, far higher conversion, and it protects your domain.

You can even use the *attributes* of your purchased list (the industry, role, or interests it was sold as) to target ads — just don't email the addresses themselves.

**Landing-page hero copy** *(Assuming a B2B software/service offer — swap in your real product, audience, and proof where bracketed):*

> ### [Specific outcome your product delivers — e.g. "Cut your monthly reporting from 2 days to 20 minutes"]
> [One line on how, and who it's for — e.g. "Automated reporting for finance teams at 10–200-person companies."]
>
> **[Get the free 5-minute demo →]**
>
> *No credit card. See it work on your own numbers.*

Drive traffic to that with ads, content, or partnerships, and email *only* the people who opt in.

## Path 2: a real cold *sales* email (1-to-1, not a blast)

There's a legal lane for genuinely cold B2B outreach: small-batch, personalized, relevant emails sent individually (the model used by tools like Apollo or Lemlist), with a real sender identity, a clear opt-out, and your physical address. The key differences from a "blast": it's targeted, personalized per recipient, sent in small volumes, and instantly stops on any reply of "no." This works for high-value B2B prospecting — not for 50,000 random consumer addresses.

If your offer fits that lane, here's a template. Personalize the bracketed bits *per recipient* — relevance is the whole game in cold email.

**Subject line — 3 angles, pick one:**
- *Direct benefit:* `[Outcome] for [their company]?`
- *Curiosity:* `quick question about [their team]'s [process]`
- *Specific observation:* `noticed [specific thing about their company]`

**Preview text:** `30 seconds, then I'll leave you alone.`

**Body** *(keep under ~120 words):*

> Hi [First name],
>
> I saw [specific, true observation about their company — a recent hire, a launch, something on their site]. Most [their role]s I talk to are stuck [specific problem your product solves], which usually costs them [concrete cost — hours, money, errors].
>
> [Your product] fixes that by [one-sentence mechanism]. [Customer like them] used it to [specific result — `[STAT: real customer outcome, cite source]`].
>
> Worth a 15-minute look? If now's not the time, just reply "not now" and I won't follow up.
>
> [Your name]
> [Title, Company]
> [Physical mailing address]
> [Unsubscribe / "reply STOP to opt out"]

**Why this version works:** it leads with *their* world, makes one specific claim backed by proof, asks for one low-friction thing, and gives an easy out — which is exactly what keeps it both effective and compliant.

> Note: I've left `[STAT: ...]` and the observation lines as placeholders on purpose — drop in *real* customer results and *real* facts about each prospect. Inventing either would break the same trust (and laws) we're trying to stay on the right side of.
