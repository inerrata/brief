## The plan

### 1. Don't translate — pick the right job per asset

| Asset | Job | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Tagline / hero headline | **Transcreation** | Its power is in the wording/feeling. Almost never literally translated. |
| Hero subhead, section headers, CTAs | **Transcreation / heavy localization** | Tone and the implied "you" register matter as much as meaning. |
| Feature descriptions, body copy | **Localization** | Adapt examples, units, workflows — but meaning stays. |
| Pricing, dates, units, legal/footer | **Localization + repricing** | ¥ pricing set to market, JST, local legal copy — not a currency conversion. |
| Docs, support, spec sheets | **Translation** | Accuracy matters, creativity doesn't. |

The mistake to avoid: running the whole page through one translation pass at one register. The hero needs a copywriter; the spec sheet needs a translator.

### 2. The formality register is a real decision — make it deliberately

Japanese forces a choice English doesn't: how polite/formal the brand speaks. For B2B SaaS the safe, credible default is **です/ます (polite-formal)** — professional without being stiff. Going casual (だ/である or breezy startup tone) can read as unserious to an enterprise buyer; going ultra-formal (humble/honorific 敬語 everywhere) can read as cold and distant. I'd recommend です/ます as the house register, with CTAs kept short and action-oriented. We lock this in the style guide so every asset matches.

### 3. Rebuild the proof and social proof for Japan

- Lead with **Japanese logos and Japanese case studies** the moment you have even 2–3. Until then, use named numeric proof ("X teams," "Y tasks tracked") and any region-relevant security/compliance marks rather than transplanting US testimonials.
- Japanese buyers value **reassurance and risk-reduction**: clear security posture, data handling, support in Japanese, and a low-risk way to try. Make "誰が使っていて、安全か" (who uses it, and is it safe) easy to answer above the fold.
- If you have ISO 27001 / SOC 2 / a Japanese-language support commitment, surface it — these do work here.

### 4. Localize the channel mix, not just the page

The home-market funnel may not be the Japan funnel:
- **Search:** Google dominates Japanese web search (Yahoo! Japan runs on Google's index), so Google SEO still matters — but **keywords must be researched fresh in Japanese**, not translated. The literal translation of "project management software" is not necessarily what Japanese buyers type (プロジェクト管理ツール, タスク管理, 工数管理 etc. have different volume and intent). Redo keyword research natively.
- **Discovery & trust:** Japanese B2B buyers lean on review/aggregator sites (e.g. ITreview, BOXIL, kyozon) and detailed comparison content far more than US buyers. Plan to be listed and reviewed there.
- **Messaging/ops:** LINE is dominant for messaging; if you do nurture or support, account for it.

### 5. Locale mechanics (the unglamorous stuff that signals "native")

- **Price to the market in ¥**, don't currency-convert. Japanese SaaS pricing conventions (per-user, annual options, tax-inclusive display, 税込/税抜 clarity, invoice/請求書 payment, bank transfer) differ from US card-default norms. Offer the payment methods Japanese businesses expect.
- **Dates:** YYYY年MM月DD日 / JST.
- **Name order & honorifics:** family name first; 様 in forms/email.
- **Layout & typography:** Japanese runs **much shorter than English**, but line-breaking, no-italics, proper Japanese web fonts (e.g. Noto Sans JP), and correct CJK rendering matter — verify no mojibake and that buttons/headers don't look sparse or broken after the text shrinks.
- **hreflang + URL structure:** set `hreflang="ja-JP"` and a clean structure (ja subdirectory/subdomain or .jp) so the right market sees the right page.

### 6. Native, in-market review is non-negotiable

Machine translation (or a non-native translator) is a *draft* at best for marketing and a liability for the hero/tagline. Before launch:
- Brief a **native Japanese B2B copywriter** with intent, audience, the one message, the register decision, and a glossary — not just strings.
- Have an **in-market reviewer** (someone who lives in the culture, not just speaks the language) check the final page.
- **Pressure-test the product name** in Japanese: is it pronounceable in katakana, does it carry an unfortunate meaning, does it collide with a domestic trademark? Check before you print it on anything.

### 7. Build a glossary + style guide once, reuse everywhere

Approved Japanese translations of your product/feature terms, the です/ます register rule, CTA conventions, do/don't words, and how much the global voice may flex in Japan. This keeps the homepage, ads, emails, and docs consistent and saves money on every future asset.
