## The big idea: transcreation, not translation

There are three ways to take copy into a new market, and only one of them works for a homepage:

| Approach | What it is | Use it for |
|---|---|---|
| **Translation** | Literal, meaning-preserving conversion | Legal text, help docs, UI strings |
| **Localization** | Translation + adapting formats, examples, references | Most marketing pages |
| **Transcreation** | Re-writing the *message* natively for the new market while keeping the strategic intent | Your homepage hero, value prop, taglines, CTAs |

Your hero section, tagline, and value proposition should be **transcreated** - written fresh in Japanese by someone who thinks in Japanese - not translated. Translate the supporting body and feature details. This is the difference between sounding like a foreign company that ran Google Translate and sounding like a company that belongs in the market.

## What is actually different about the Japanese B2B SaaS buyer

These six differences should drive every copy and design decision:

1. **Trust before benefit.** Western homepages lead with the outcome ("Ship projects 2x faster"). Japanese B2B buyers typically need to feel the company is *credible and safe* before they care about the upside. Logos, company registration details, named customers, awards, security/compliance, and a real Japanese phone number and address carry disproportionate weight. Risk-avoidance often outweighs upside-seeking.

2. **Consensus buying (ringi / 稟議).** Purchases are rarely made by one champion. A team builds consensus and routes an approval document upward. Your page is not persuading one person - it is arming an internal champion to sell *internally*. That means you need shareable proof: ROI framing, security documentation, comparison material, and case studies they can forward.

3. **Density is trust, not clutter.** The minimalist, white-space-heavy Western hero can read as "thin" or "unserious" to Japanese audiences. Successful Japanese SaaS pages are typically denser - more information above the fold, more proof, more navigation. Do not blindly maximize white space.

4. **Service and support are part of the product.** Onboarding support, a responsive Japanese-language help desk, and "we will hold your hand" reassurance are major purchase drivers. Surface support prominently - it is not an afterthought here.

5. **Politeness level (keigo / 敬語) and tone.** B2B copy uses polite/formal register (desu-masu / ですます調), but the best modern SaaS brands keep it warm and clear rather than stiff. Getting register right is a native-speaker job; tone-deaf formality (or inappropriate casualness) signals "outsider" instantly.

6. **English loanwords vs. native terms.** Some tech terms are commonly used in katakana English (タスク, プロジェクト, ダッシュボード); others should be native Japanese. A native marketer will know which is which - guessing here makes you sound off.

## Homepage section-by-section localization guide

I have laid out each homepage block, what to do, and **placeholder Japanese transcreations** you can react to. Treat the Japanese below as *directional drafts to validate with a native marketer*, not final shippable copy - I would want your real source copy and a native review before anything goes live.

### 1. Hero (headline + subhead + primary CTA)
- **Do not** literally translate a punchy English tagline. Re-write for the outcome that matters most to Japanese teams, framed with reassurance.
- Lead the subhead with proof of safety/credibility (e.g. "Used by X companies," "ISO/SOC 2," "fully Japanese support").

*Directional example (adjust once I have your real value prop):*
> **Headline / 見出し:** チームの仕事を、ひとつの場所に。
> *("Your team's work, all in one place.")*
> **Subhead / サブ見出し:** 導入から運用まで日本語で手厚くサポート。〇〇社が利用するプロジェクト管理ツール。
> *("Hands-on support in Japanese from setup to daily use. The project-management tool used by [N] companies.")*
> **CTA:** 無料で試す *("Try free")* / or 資料ダウンロード *("Download materials") - see CTA note below.*

### 2. Primary CTA strategy - this is a real difference
In Japan, **"資料請求 / 資料ダウンロード" (request/download materials)** is often a *stronger* primary CTA than "Start free trial." Many buyers want a PDF to circulate internally (for the ringi process) before they ever sign up. Recommendation: run **two CTAs** - a soft conversion (download materials / book a demo) as the primary, and the free trial as secondary. Test which converts.

### 3. Social proof / logos
- Move customer logos higher than you would in the US. Prioritize **recognizable Japanese companies** if you have any - even one or two domestic logos dramatically outperforms ten Western logos.
- If you have no Japanese customers yet, lead instead with hard credibility: security certifications, user counts, uptime, parent-company backing, or named global enterprises that Japanese buyers respect.

### 4. Features / benefits
- Keep one core message; translate feature details faithfully but **re-order by what Japanese teams prioritize** (often: ease of adoption, support, security, integration with tools they already use).
- Replace US-centric examples, screenshots with English UI, and integrations that are not used in Japan. Show the **Japanese product UI** in screenshots - an English-only screenshot undercuts the whole page.

### 5. Pricing
- Show prices in **JPY (¥)**, and clearly note 税抜/税込 (excl./incl. consumption tax).
- Japanese buyers often expect to talk to sales for team/enterprise tiers - an "お問い合わせ / お見積もり" (contact us / request a quote) option is expected alongside self-serve.

### 6. Trust and support block (elevate this)
- Add a prominent block covering: Japanese-language support hours, onboarding assistance, security/compliance, a Japanese company address and phone number, and ideally a Japanese case study.
- This block often *is* the conversion driver in Japan. Do not bury it in the footer.

### 7. Footer and legalese
- Add 特定商取引法 (Specified Commercial Transactions Act) disclosures if you sell directly online - many Japanese SaaS sites include a 特商法 page, and buyers look for it as a trust signal.
- Localize date formats (YYYY年MM月DD日), currency, and ensure the privacy policy reflects Japan's APPI (個人情報保護法 / Act on the Protection of Personal Information).

## Beyond the homepage: what a Japan launch needs (so the page actually converts)

The homepage will not perform in a vacuum. Priorities, roughly in order:

1. **A native Japanese marketer/copywriter to own transcreation and review.** This is the highest-leverage hire/contractor. Machine translation or non-native copy is the #1 reason foreign SaaS stalls in Japan.
2. **Japanese-language support and onboarding** - even part-time at launch. It is a purchase driver, not just post-sale.
3. **Localized product UI and key docs.** A Japanese homepage leading to an English-only product loses trust fast.
4. **A downloadable materials PDF (サービス資料)** for the ringi/consensus process - often more important than the trial.
5. **Local trust infrastructure:** Japanese entity/address/phone, security certs surfaced, 特商法 page, JPY billing, and ideally invoice-based payment (請求書払い), which many Japanese companies prefer over credit cards.
6. **Channel reality:** Japanese B2B buyers research heavily and use comparison/review sites (e.g. ITreview, BOXIL, and similar aggregators). Plan for presence there. SEO needs native Japanese keyword research - translated English keywords do not map to how people actually search.
