**Short answer: No — generally it is not legal to secretly record a phone call in California without the other party''s consent.**

California is a "two-party consent" (more precisely, **all-party consent**) state. Under the California Invasion of Privacy Act (**Penal Code § 632**), it is illegal to record a "confidential communication" — including a phone call — unless **everyone** on the call has consented.

### Key points

- **All parties must consent.** If even one participant on a confidential call hasn''t agreed, recording it without telling them is unlawful.
- **It applies to "confidential communications"** — conversations where at least one party has a reasonable expectation that no one is secretly listening or recording. A private phone call typically qualifies. Conversations clearly held in public, where there''s no reasonable expectation of privacy, may not.
- **Consent doesn''t have to be a signed form.** It can be express or implied. A common practice is an audible beep tone or a spoken disclosure ("this call may be recorded"). If the other person continues the call after a clear notice, that generally counts as consent.
- **Cell and cordless calls** are specifically covered (Penal Code §§ 632.5–632.7), and recording them without consent carries its own penalties.

### Consequences of recording without consent

- **Criminal penalties:** Up to a **$2,500 fine per violation** and/or up to **one year in jail** (it can be charged as a misdemeanor or felony).
- **Civil liability:** The recorded person can sue for the greater of **$5,000 per violation or three times actual damages** (Penal Code § 637.2).
- **Evidence problems:** Illegally recorded calls are generally **inadmissible** in California court proceedings.

### Cross-state calls add complexity

If you''re in California and the other person is in a **one-party consent** state (where only one party needs to agree), the law gets murky. Courts have often applied the **stricter** law — meaning California''s all-party rule can still govern. The safest approach is to assume all-party consent is required.

### Practical takeaway

If you want to record a California phone call legally, the simplest and safest path is to **tell the other person you''re recording and get their agreement on the recording itself** (e.g., "I''d like to record this call — is that okay?"). Their verbal "yes," captured at the start, gives you clear consent.
