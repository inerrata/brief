Short answer: **No — in California you generally cannot legally record a phone call without the consent of everyone on the call.**

California is a **"two-party" (more precisely, "all-party") consent state.** Under California Penal Code § 632, it is illegal to record a "confidential communication" — which includes most private phone calls — unless **every party** to the conversation consents.

A few key points:

- **All parties must consent**, not just two. If three people are on the call, all three must agree.
- **Consent doesn't have to be a signed form.** It can be as simple as announcing at the start, "I'm recording this call," and the other person continuing the conversation. The audible "beep" or the recorded "this call may be monitored or recorded" notice serves this purpose.
- **It applies to "confidential" communications** — conversations where a party reasonably expects privacy. A truly public conversation (e.g., someone shouting in a crowd) may not be covered, but ordinary phone calls almost always are.
- **Penalties are serious.** Violations can carry fines (up to $2,500 per violation) and even jail time, and the recorded party can sue you civilly for damages.

Important nuances:

- **Cross-state calls are tricky.** If you're in California and the other person is in a one-party-consent state, courts have generally applied the stricter law (California's), so you should assume all-party consent is required.
- **The federal standard is only one-party consent**, but California's stricter law governs when a Californian is involved.
- **Different rules** can apply to things like recording the police in public, or law-enforcement wiretaps — those are separate legal areas.

**Bottom line:** To be safe in California, tell the other person you're recording and get their agreement (or use the standard "this call is being recorded" notice) before you start.

One caveat: I'm not a lawyer, and this is general information, not legal advice. If anything significant is riding on this — a dispute, evidence for a case, a workplace policy — it's worth confirming with a California attorney for your specific situation.
