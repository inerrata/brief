# Is a $400 CAC Good at $50/month with 18-Month Retention?

Yes, this is a workable unit economics profile. Here's the breakdown:

## The Math

- **LTV (Lifetime Value)** = $50/month × 18 months = **$900**
- **CAC** = **$400**
- **LTV:CAC ratio** = $900 ÷ $400 = **2.25:1**
- **CAC Payback Period** = $400 ÷ $50 = **8 months**

## What These Numbers Mean

**LTV:CAC of 2.25:1:** A common benchmark for healthy subscription businesses is 3:1. You're below that threshold, but still profitable per customer — you make $500 back on every $400 spent. The gap to 3:1 means there's room to improve, either by reducing CAC or increasing LTV.

**Payback period of 8 months:** You recover your acquisition cost in 8 months. This is reasonable for most subscription businesses, though it can strain cash flow if you're growing fast or operating lean.

## Important caveat: use gross margin, not revenue

If your gross margin is less than 100% (it almost certainly is), the real picture is tighter:
- At 70% margin: margin-adjusted LTV = $630, LTV:CAC = 1.58:1
- At 80% margin: margin-adjusted LTV = $720, LTV:CAC = 1.80:1

For software, margins are often 70–90%, so you're likely still profitable per customer. For physical products or service-heavy businesses, do the margin-adjusted math.

## Bottom Line

Your $400 CAC is not a problem at these numbers — it's a foundation to build on. To strengthen it, focus on:
1. Reducing churn (18 months → 20 months = $100 more LTV per customer)
2. Expanding revenue from existing customers (upsells, add-ons)
3. Lowering CAC through better conversion rates or channel mix
