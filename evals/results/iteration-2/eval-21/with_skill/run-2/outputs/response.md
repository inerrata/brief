# Your Launch Plan: Notion Template Business, No Audience, One Month Out

You have one month, a paid product, and zero audience. Here's the plan — structured around what actually works when you're starting from zero.

---

## The honest framing

Without an audience, your biggest risk is launching to silence. The goal of the next 30 days isn't just to launch — it's to build a small, relevant audience before launch day so you're not yelling into a void.

---

## Week 1–2: Find and get in front of your buyers before you sell anything

Your templates solve a specific problem for a specific type of person. Name that person precisely. ("People who use Notion" is too broad. "Freelancers who invoice and track clients in Notion" or "students building a second-brain in Notion" is a buyer.)

**Where they already are:**
- Reddit: r/Notion, r/productivity, r/freelance, r/GetMotivated (match to your template's use case)
- Twitter/X: search for people posting about Notion workflows
- Notion community forums and template galleries
- Facebook Groups around productivity, Notion, or your niche
- YouTube: people who watch Notion tutorials are buyers

**What to do this week:**
1. Join 3–5 of these communities and genuinely participate. Answer questions. Don't sell.
2. Share a free tip, mini-template, or framework that solves a small version of the problem your paid template solves. No pitch.
3. Collect reactions: what problems are people most vocal about? What gets saves/upvotes?

**Start a waitlist.** Build a simple landing page (Gumroad, Lemon Squeezy, or Carrd) describing what you're building and let people express interest. "Get notified when it launches — plus an early-bird discount" is enough.

---

## Week 2–3: Create evidence that the template works

You need proof before launch. Without it, buyers hesitate.

- Give the template to 3–5 people for free in exchange for honest feedback and a testimonial. Target people from the communities you joined.
- Get a screenshot, a short quote, or a before/after result from each tester.
- These become your social proof on launch day.

**Content to publish this week:**
- 1–2 posts showing the template in use (a Loom walkthrough, a screenshot of a real workflow, a before/after of someone's Notion setup)
- Post in the communities where you've been active. "I built this for [specific problem] — here's what it looks like."

---

## Week 3–4: Pre-launch and launch

**Pre-launch (3–5 days before):**
- Email your waitlist (even if it's 20 people). Tell them what you're launching, why it exists, and give them first access + a discount (e.g., 30% off for 48 hours).
- Post a "launching in 3 days" teaser in your communities.
- DM anyone who engaged meaningfully with your earlier posts to let them know.

**Launch day:**
- Post in all relevant communities simultaneously. Lead with the problem, show the template, include social proof.
- Email your waitlist: "it's live."
- Post on Twitter/X with a short demo.
- Submit to Notion template directories and curation accounts (several exist on Twitter and Instagram).
- Be online to respond to comments and questions.

**Post-launch (days 2–7):**
- Follow up with non-buyers: "Here's a question I got from someone who was on the fence..." — addresses objections.
- Share early customer reactions ("someone just set this up and said...").
- Keep posting value content — don't go silent after day 1.

---

## What to measure

- Waitlist signups (leading indicator of launch interest)
- Launch-day and week-1 sales
- Traffic to the sales page vs. conversion rate (if few sales: is traffic low, or is the page not converting?)
- Where buyers came from (ask them, or use UTM links on each community post)

---

## One thing to focus on above everything else

One month is short. You cannot be everywhere. Pick the single community/platform where your exact buyer is most concentrated, and go deep there — consistent, genuinely helpful presence. One community done well beats five communities done thin.

---

*Adjust the above based on what your templates are actually for — the specific niche changes which communities matter most.*
