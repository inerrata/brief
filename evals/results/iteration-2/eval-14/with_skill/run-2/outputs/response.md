# How to Get Your Blog Post About Remote Team Onboarding to Rank on Google

Here's a step-by-step approach grounded in how search actually works.

---

## 1. Match the search intent first

Before optimizing anything, search your target keyword and look at what's ranking. For "remote team onboarding," the top results are likely guides and how-to articles — informational intent. Your post needs to match that format. If you're writing a short 500-word opinion piece and the top results are 2,500-word step-by-step guides, you'll lose on format alone before a single ranking factor is considered.

---

## 2. Target a specific, rankable keyword

"Remote team onboarding" is a head term — broad, high-competition, and hard to crack unless your site already has authority. Consider more specific long-tail variants you can realistically win:

- "remote team onboarding checklist"
- "how to onboard remote employees"
- "remote onboarding best practices for managers"
- "onboarding remote employees first week"

These have lower volume but higher intent and lower competition. Use a tool like Ahrefs, Semrush, or even Google Search Console (if your site is established) to find the version with a realistic difficulty score for your domain authority.

**Action:** Pick one primary keyword. Write for that keyword and build everything else around it.

---

## 3. Optimize on-page elements

Once you have your keyword:

- **Title tag:** Include the primary keyword, keep it under ~60 characters, and make it compelling to click — it's your ad in the search results. Example: *"How to Onboard Remote Employees: A Step-by-Step Guide"*
- **H1:** Should match or closely mirror your title tag.
- **First 100 words:** Use the primary keyword naturally in the opening paragraph.
- **H2 subheadings:** Include the keyword or close variants in at least one H2. Structure headings around the questions your reader needs answered.
- **Meta description:** 150–160 characters. Include the keyword (it bolds in results) and give a reason to click — a specific benefit or what they'll get.
- **URL slug:** Short, keyword-forward. Example: `/remote-team-onboarding-guide`

---

## 4. Make the content genuinely better than what ranks

Google's job is to surface the most useful result. To beat what's already ranking, your post needs to be *meaningfully* better — not just present. Ways to differentiate:

- Include a concrete checklist or template readers can copy
- Add a section on the first-week schedule or day-one agenda (searchers frequently ask this)
- Pull from real experience or cite specific research on remote work outcomes
- Answer the "People Also Ask" questions Google surfaces for the term — they signal what searchers want to know

---

## 5. Answer common related questions

Look at the "People Also Ask" box on Google for your keyword. Common questions around remote onboarding include:
- How long should remote onboarding take?
- What tools do remote teams use for onboarding?
- How do you build culture with remote new hires?

Address these within your post. Each one is a potential featured snippet and signals to Google that your content covers the topic comprehensively.

---

## 6. Build internal links

Link to this post from other relevant pages on your site, and link out from this post to related content you've already written. Use descriptive anchor text (e.g., "remote onboarding checklist" not "click here"). This spreads authority and helps Google understand your content's context.

---

## 7. Build backlinks (the hard part)

On-page optimization alone won't rank a competitive post. You need other sites to link to it. Practical approaches:
- Share it with communities where remote work managers gather (Slack groups, LinkedIn, relevant subreddits)
- Reach out to authors of round-up posts on remote work tools or practices and suggest your post as a resource
- Link to it from any guest posts you write elsewhere

---

## 8. E-E-A-T signals

Google weights Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trust. Add an author bio showing relevant experience, cite credible sources, and include first-hand observations or original data if you have them. This matters more for topics where credibility is at stake.

---

## Quick checklist before you publish

- [ ] Primary keyword in title, H1, first 100 words, at least one H2, and meta description
- [ ] Title tag under 60 characters and click-worthy
- [ ] Meta description 150–160 characters with keyword and a reason to click
- [ ] Content matches informational intent and is structured like a guide
- [ ] Covers the topic comprehensively enough that readers don't need another result
- [ ] Internal links to and from relevant existing content
- [ ] Author bio / E-E-A-T signals present

---

**One thing to check today:** Search your target keyword, look at the top 3 results, and note their structure, word count, and the questions they answer. If your post doesn't cover all of those and more, extend it before publishing.
