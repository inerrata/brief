# How to Get Your Blog Post About Remote Team Onboarding to Rank on Google

Here are the most important things to focus on:

## 1. Target the right keyword

"Remote team onboarding" is competitive. You'll have better luck with specific long-tail variations like:
- "how to onboard remote employees"
- "remote employee onboarding checklist"
- "remote onboarding best practices"

Search each phrase on Google and look at what's already ranking. If you see big authority sites dominating (LinkedIn, Forbes, major HR platforms), consider narrowing further — for example, "remote onboarding checklist for small teams" or "remote onboarding software engineers."

## 2. Match the format of what's already ranking

Search your target keyword and look at the top results. Are they long-form guides? Listicles? Checklists? Your post needs to match that format, or Google won't rank it for that intent. If everyone ranking is doing a 10-step guide with subheadings, write a 10-step guide with subheadings.

## 3. On-page basics

- Put your keyword in the **title tag** (keep it under 60 characters)
- Use the keyword in your **H1** and in the **first paragraph**
- Use related phrases in your **H2 subheadings**
- Write a **meta description** (150–160 characters) that includes the keyword and gives a reason to click
- Use a clean **URL slug** like `/remote-team-onboarding-guide`

## 4. Make it genuinely useful and comprehensive

Google rewards content that fully satisfies the search. That means:
- Cover every angle the searcher might need — tools, first-week schedule, common mistakes, cultural integration, etc.
- Look at the "People Also Ask" box for your keyword and answer those questions in your post
- Add something original: a downloadable checklist, a first-week schedule template, or your own experience running remote onboarding

## 5. Build internal links

Link to your blog post from other pages on your site that are related. Also link out from the post to other relevant content you've written. Use descriptive anchor text.

## 6. Get backlinks

This is the hardest part. Without links from other sites pointing to your post, you'll struggle to rank for anything competitive. Options:
- Share in relevant communities (LinkedIn groups, Slack communities for HR, People Ops, remote work)
- Reach out to blogs that have already written about remote work and suggest your post as a reference
- Guest post on related sites and link back to your article

## 7. Be patient

New posts rarely rank immediately. Give it 3–6 months, monitor it in Google Search Console, and update it if needed based on what search terms it's starting to get impressions for.

The single biggest lever: make your post the most useful, complete resource on the topic. If it's genuinely better than what ranks, Google will find it eventually.
