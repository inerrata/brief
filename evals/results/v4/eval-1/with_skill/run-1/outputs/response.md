Building a founder's LinkedIn audience from zero is one of the highest-leverage things a B2B startup can do — in payroll especially, where trust and "do these people actually understand the pain of running payroll" is most of the sale. Here's how I'd approach it, then a batch of post ideas you can ship this week.

*Assuming: the founder is comfortable being the face (LinkedIn audiences follow people, not logos), the ICP is finance/ops/HR leaders or founders at SMBs who run payroll, and the goal is reach + trust now, pipeline later. Adjust any of these and I'll re-aim.*

---

## The approach

**1. Pick the founder, not the company page.**
People follow people. A company page on LinkedIn is a graveyard for a startup with no audience — organic reach is dismal. The founder's personal profile is the engine. The company page exists, you cross-post the occasional milestone, but 95% of the effort goes into the founder's feed.

**2. Fix the profile before posting a single thing.**
The profile is the landing page every post drives traffic to. Before growth starts:
- **Headline:** not "CEO at [Startup]." Make it about who you help and how. e.g. *"Helping [SMB founders / finance teams] run payroll without the dread | Building [Company]"*
- **Banner + about section:** one clear line on what you're building and who it's for. A visitor should know in 5 seconds whether to follow.
- **Featured section:** pin your best post or a useful free resource once you have one.

**3. Commit to a cadence you can actually hold.**
Consistency beats volume. **3 posts a week for a year** beats daily for three weeks then silence. The algorithm rewards regularity, and so does the reader who needs to see you 3–4 times before they follow. Pick a sustainable rhythm and protect it.

**4. Build around 3–4 content pillars.**
Pillars are the recurring themes you want to become known for. They sit where *what your audience cares about* meets *what you have genuine authority on* meets *what drives your business*. For a payroll founder I'd suggest:

| Pillar | What it covers | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| **Payroll horror stories & fixes** | The mistakes, compliance traps, and disasters you've seen — and how to avoid them | High-stakes, relatable pain; finance people *fear* getting payroll wrong |
| **Building in public** | Real numbers, decisions, failures, and lessons from building the company | Highest-trust format; humanizes you and compounds over time |
| **Contrarian takes on the category** | Where you disagree with how payroll/HR software is built or sold | Invites debate = reach; positions you as a sharp insider |
| **Genuinely useful how-to** | Practical guides: classifying contractors, multi-state tax, year-end close | Saveable, shareable, signals "this person knows their stuff" |

A rough mix: ~60% useful/educational, ~25% story/build-in-public, ~15% direct asks or product mentions. If everything is promotional, people tune out; if nothing is, you never convert.

**5. The first line is 80% of the work.**
On LinkedIn only the first ~210 characters show before "...see more." That line decides whether anyone reads the rest. Front-load the payoff, never open with throat-clearing ("I've been thinking lately about…"). Short lines, generous white space, one idea per post.

**6. Engagement is the actual growth channel — not posting.**
For a brand-new account, the single most underrated tactic is **thoughtful comments on bigger accounts in your space**. Spend 15 minutes a day leaving real, additive replies on posts from payroll/HR/fintech voices and your ICP's favorite creators. That's where a from-zero account borrows reach. Also: reply to every comment on your own posts in the first hour — early velocity drives distribution.

**7. Mechanics that matter on LinkedIn specifically.**
- Put any **link in the first comment**, not the post body (in-body links suppress reach).
- A few relevant tags are fine; a wall of 30 hashtags reads as spam and tanks reach.
- Carousels (PDF documents) and personal-story-to-lesson posts are the formats that travel here.
- No engagement bait ("comment YES below"). Platforms demote it and your ICP distrusts it.

## 8 post ideas to start with

These map to the pillars above. Each leads with a hook written to survive the "see more" cutoff.

**1. Payroll horror story (the cold open)**
> A 12-person company once paid an employee $43,000 instead of $4,300.
>
> Nobody caught it for 9 days.
>
> Here's the boring process failure that caused it — and the 2-minute check that would've stopped it.

*Why it works: specific number, real stakes, and a useful payoff. This is the format that builds your reputation fastest.*

**2. Contrarian take**
> Most payroll software isn't built for the person who actually runs payroll.
>
> It's built to demo well to the person who *buys* it.
>
> That gap is why your finance team quietly hates the tool you picked. Let me explain.

*Why it works: challenges a belief, picks a side your ICP secretly agrees with, invites debate.*

**3. Build-in-public / numbers**
> We just lost our second-biggest customer. Here's exactly why, and what we're changing.

*Then: the real reason (honestly), what you learned, what you're doing differently. Transparency compounds trust like nothing else. Only post numbers/details you're comfortable being public.*

**4. Genuinely useful how-to (carousel)**
> Hiring your first contractor in another state? Here are the 5 things that will get you fined if you skip them.

*Build as a PDF carousel, one trap per slide. Saveable and shareable — exactly what grows a from-zero account.*

**5. "I was wrong about X"**
> For a year I thought our biggest competitor was [BigCo].
>
> I was wrong. Our real competitor is a spreadsheet and a prayer.
>
> Here's what that taught us about who we're actually selling to.

*Why it works: admitting a reversed belief has a high stop rate because it reads as honest, not promotional.*

**6. Relatable frustration**
> Nothing makes a founder feel less like a founder than realizing payroll runs on Friday and you're not sure the numbers are right.

*Then: the systems-level reason this happens to small teams and how to de-risk it. Names a feeling your ICP has had — instant recognition.*

**7. Direct call-out of the audience**
> If you're a founder running payroll yourself to "save money," read this before your next run.

*Then a short, genuinely useful list of the hidden costs (compliance risk, your time, error exposure). Useful first, product implied — not pitched.*

**8. The origin story (pinned-worthy)**
> I started a payroll company because I watched a friend's business almost die over a tax-filing mistake nobody warned them about.

*The personal "why" behind the company. This is the post that makes strangers care about you, not just your product. Strong candidate for your Featured section.*
