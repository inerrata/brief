# Building Your Founder's LinkedIn Audience From Zero

Here's a practical, no-fluff approach to building your founder's LinkedIn presence as a B2B payroll startup, plus post ideas you can ship this week.

---

## The Strategy

### 1. Get the positioning right first
Your founder isn't building a personal brand for vanity — they're building **distribution for the company**. The goal is for the right people (finance leaders, HR/People ops, ops-heavy founders, agencies, accountants) to associate your founder's name with "the person who actually understands payroll pain."

Pick a **narrow point of view**. "Payroll" is too broad. Niche down to something like:
- Payroll for fast-growing startups / multi-state teams
- Payroll for companies with contractors + full-time blend
- The hidden cost of getting payroll wrong (compliance, penalties, employee trust)

A specific lens is what makes a feed-stopping post. Generic "payroll is important" content gets ignored.

### 2. Optimize the profile before posting (this is the conversion layer)
Before a single post, fix the founder's profile, because every post drives profile visits:
- **Headline**: Not "CEO at [Company]." Use the outcome + audience. E.g. *"Helping startups run payroll without the compliance landmines | Building [Company]"*
- **Banner**: One clear line on what you do and who for.
- **Featured section**: Pin a lead magnet (a checklist, a teardown, a short guide) or your best post.
- **About section**: Write it in first person, lead with the problem you obsess over, end with a soft CTA (DM me / book a call / try the product).

### 3. Post cadence and format
- **3–4 posts/week** is the sweet spot for growth without burnout. Consistency beats volume.
- **Text + single image or native document (carousel)** outperforms links. LinkedIn suppresses posts with external links in the body — put links in the **first comment** instead.
- Best windows for B2B: roughly **Tue–Thu, 8–10am** in your audience's main time zone. Test and adjust.
- Hook is everything. The first 1–2 lines decide whether anyone clicks "see more." Lead with tension, a contrarian take, or a specific number.

### 4. The growth engine isn't posting — it's engagement
This is where most founders fail. Posting into the void does nothing at zero followers. Do this daily:
- **Comment thoughtfully on 5–10 posts/day** from people in your space (other founders, fractional CFOs, HR influencers, accountants). Smart comments on big accounts borrow their audience.
- **Reply to every comment** on your own posts within the first hour — early engagement signals the algorithm to push your post wider.
- **Build a small "engagement pod"** of 5–10 peers who reliably comment on each other's posts in the first 30 minutes. Keep it genuine, not spammy.

### 5. Content pillars (rotate these)
Give the founder 3–4 buckets so they never stare at a blank page:
1. **Educational** — demystify payroll/compliance (multi-state taxes, contractor classification, pay-run mechanics).
2. **Contrarian / POV** — challenge how the industry does things; spark debate.
3. **Behind-the-scenes / build-in-public** — the startup journey, lessons, mistakes, metrics. This humanizes the founder and is the #1 follower magnet for early-stage.
4. **Customer/proof** — anonymized stories, before/after, results, mistakes you've seen.

### 6. Measure what matters
At zero, vanity metrics lie. Track:
- **Follower growth + profile views** (audience building)
- **Comments + DMs** (the real signal — conversations, not likes)
- **Demo requests / waitlist signups attributed to LinkedIn** (the business outcome)

Give it **90 days** of consistency before judging. Audience compounds slowly then suddenly.

## Post Ideas (ready to adapt)

**1. The expensive mistake hook (Educational)**
> "A 12-person startup just paid $14,000 in penalties because they got one payroll thing wrong.
>
> They hired their second employee in another state and assumed payroll 'just worked' across state lines. It doesn't. Here's what actually happens when you hire across state lines — and the 4 things to set up before that first paycheck runs..."

**2. The contrarian take (POV)**
> "Unpopular opinion: most startups shouldn't run payroll in-house — and most also shouldn't fully outsource it.
>
> The founders who get this right do a third thing. Here's the setup I'd recommend if you're under 50 people..."

**3. Build-in-public (Behind-the-scenes)**
> "We almost built the wrong product.
>
> When we started [Company], we thought the payroll pain was [X]. After 30 customer calls, the real pain was something nobody talks about. Here's what we learned — and what we tore up..."

**4. The teardown / checklist (Educational, high-save)**
> "The 7-point payroll checklist every startup should run before their next pay cycle. Save this.
> 1. ...
> 2. ...
> (Make it a native carousel/document — these get the most saves and follows.)"

**5. The myth-buster (POV)**
> "'Payroll is just paying people on time.' No — that's the easy 20%.
>
> The other 80% is the stuff that quietly creates compliance risk, employee distrust, and finance headaches. Let me break down the part nobody warns you about..."

**6. The relatable pain post (Educational + human)**
> "The most stressful 48 hours for any startup founder isn't fundraising.
>
> It's the first time you run payroll for real employees and realize one wrong number means a teammate doesn't make rent. Here's how to never feel that fear again..."

**7. The proof/story post (Customer)**
> "A customer switched to us after their old provider missed a tax filing — twice.
>
> Here's the (anonymized) story of what went wrong, what it cost them, and the one process change that fixed it..."

## Your First 2 Weeks (concrete plan)

- **Day 1–2:** Rewrite the profile (headline, banner, about, featured).
- **Day 3:** Publish post #3 (build-in-public) — founder-journey posts are the easiest first win at zero followers.
- **Daily from day 1:** Comment on 5–10 relevant posts. This is non-negotiable and matters more than your own posts early on.
- **Week 1:** Post #1, #3, #6 (mix education + human).
- **Week 2:** Post #2, #4, #5 (POV + a saveable carousel).
- **End of week 2:** Look at which 1–2 posts got the most comments/DMs. Double down on that angle.
