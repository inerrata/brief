# Google Ads vs. Facebook Ads for Your Ergonomic Office Chair

Short answer: **Start with Google Search Ads to capture people already shopping for a chair, then layer in Facebook/Instagram once you have proof of what converts.** But the right mix depends on a few things about your specific situation. Here's how to think it through.

## The core difference

| | Google Ads | Facebook/Instagram Ads |
|---|---|---|
| **Intent** | High — people search "ergonomic office chair," they already want one | Low to moderate — you interrupt people scrolling; you create the desire |
| **Best for** | Capturing existing demand | Generating new demand, visual storytelling |
| **Targeting** | Keywords (what people want *right now*) | Demographics, interests, behaviors, lookalikes |
| **Creative** | Text + Shopping product images | Strong visuals, video, lifestyle imagery |
| **Typical cost** | Higher cost-per-click, but warmer traffic | Lower CPC, but more clicks needed to convert |
| **Sales cycle fit** | Great for "ready to buy" | Great for awareness + retargeting |

An office chair is a **considered purchase** ($150–$1,500+) — people research before buying. That has implications for both channels.

## Why I'd lead with Google

1. **You're selling a product people actively search for.** "Ergonomic office chair," "best office chair for back pain," "office chair under $300" are all high-intent queries. Google lets you show up exactly when someone has their wallet out.
2. **Faster path to revenue and learning.** Intent traffic converts sooner, so you learn your true cost-per-acquisition and which messages/price points work — faster than you would building demand from scratch.
3. **Google Shopping is a strong fit.** For a physical product, Shopping ads (with photo, price, reviews) often outperform text ads and have lower friction. Set up a Merchant Center feed.

**Watch out for:** Google clicks for furniture keywords can be expensive and competitive (big retailers bid heavily). Use specific long-tail and "pain point" keywords (e.g., "ergonomic chair for lower back pain," "office chair for tall people") where competition and cost are lower and intent is sharper.

## Why Facebook still matters

1. **Your product is visual and lifestyle-driven.** A great chair photographs and films well — show it in a real home office, highlight the lumbar support, the adjustability, someone sitting comfortably for 8 hours. Facebook/Instagram reward that.
2. **It builds demand Google can't.** Many people don't search for a new chair until something prompts them. A scroll-stopping ad about back pain or "your $40 chair is wrecking your posture" creates the want.
3. **Retargeting is where it shines.** Because chairs are a considered purchase, most visitors won't buy on the first visit. Retargeting site visitors and cart-abandoners on Facebook/Instagram is often the **highest-ROI spend on either platform.** Don't skip this.

## My recommended sequence (lean budget)

**Phase 1 — Validate demand (first ~$500–1,500 / 2–4 weeks)**
- Google Search + Shopping on tight, high-intent keywords.
- Goal: confirm people convert and learn your baseline CPA.

**Phase 2 — Add retargeting**
- Install the Meta Pixel and Google tag *now*, before you spend a dollar, so you're collecting audience data from day one.
- Once you have traffic, run Facebook/Instagram retargeting to people who visited but didn't buy.

**Phase 3 — Scale demand generation**
- Once you know your numbers, add Facebook/Instagram prospecting (cold audiences) with strong lifestyle creative and lookalike audiences built from your buyers.

## How to decide the split for YOUR situation

The honest answer depends on details I don't have. A few questions to settle it:

- **What's your monthly ad budget?** Under ~$1,500/mo, I'd concentrate on Google + retargeting rather than spreading thin across both at full scale.
- **What's your price point and margin?** Premium chairs ($500+) can absorb higher CPCs and benefit from Facebook's storytelling; budget chairs need cheaper traffic and volume.
- **Do you have strong photo/video creative?** Facebook lives or dies on creative. If you don't have great visuals yet, Google is the safer first bet.
- **Is there anything distinctive about the chair?** A unique hook (a novel mechanism, a specific niche like "for gamers" or "for petite users," a sustainability angle) makes Facebook demand-gen far more effective.
- **Are you selling direct (your own site) or via a marketplace like Amazon?** That changes the platform math significantly.

## Bottom line

Don't think of it as either/or — think of it as **sequence and proportion.** Lead with **Google Search + Shopping** to capture existing demand and learn fast. Run **Facebook/Instagram retargeting** from day one to recover the considered-purchase shoppers who don't buy immediately. Then **scale into Facebook prospecting** once you know your numbers.
