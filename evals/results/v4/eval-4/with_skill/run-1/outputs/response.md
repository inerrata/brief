Short answer: **start with Google Search ads, not Facebook** — but only if you've already nailed two things first. Here's the reasoning and a concrete plan.

## Why Google first for this product

People already search for "ergonomic office chair," "best chair for back pain," "lumbar support office chair." That means **demand already exists** — your job is to be the answer, not to convince someone they want one. Search captures intent that's already there, which is the fastest, cleanest signal you can buy. You'll learn whether your offer and page convert within days, not weeks.

Facebook/Instagram (and TikTok) **create** demand by interrupting people who weren't shopping. They can absolutely work for a chair — it's a visual product, and good lifestyle/demo creative travels — but they're better as your *second* channel once Search is profitable and you understand your numbers. Asking cold social audiences to drop $300+ on a chair from a brand they've never heard of is the hardest version of paid media.

**Rule of thumb:** if there's search volume for your category (there is, for office chairs), start with Search. Add paid social to scale once you have a converting page and known economics.

## Before you spend a dollar — three gates

Channel choice matters far less than these. Don't launch until all three are true:

1. **Know your target CPA.** What can you afford to pay to get one sale? Rough math: if your chair's gross margin (price minus cost of goods) is, say, $150 and you want to keep acquisition to about a third of that, your ceiling is ~$50 to acquire a customer. Set this number *before* you spend, or you'll spend fast and learn nothing.
2. **Make sure the product page converts.** Paid traffic amplifies your page — it doesn't fix a weak one. Getting your page from 2% → 4% conversion *halves* your effective CPA before you touch an ad account. For a chair specifically: strong photos from multiple angles, dimensions/weight capacity, lumbar and adjustment details, reviews/ratings, shipping + return/warranty terms, and a trust-building return window (a 30-day risk-free trial does a lot of the selling for furniture people can't sit in first).
3. **Install and test conversion tracking** (Google Ads tag + GA4, and the Meta pixel for later) *before* launch. If you can't attribute a sale to a campaign, you're flying blind.

## A concrete starting plan

**Phase 1 — Google Search (weeks 1–4)**
- One campaign, tightly themed ad groups: "ergonomic office chair," "office chair back pain / lumbar support," and one for any branded/competitor terms. Keep each ad group's keywords tight so the ad matches the query.
- Bid strategy: start on Maximize Conversions, move to Target CPA once you have ~15–30 conversions of data feeding it.
- Send clicks to the product/landing page, not your homepage.
- Add **retargeting** (cheap Google Display or Meta) for people who visited but didn't buy — for a considered purchase like a chair, this is where a lot of the closes happen.

**Phase 2 — Paid social (once Search is profitable)**
- Then layer in Meta. Lead with the *problem* ("back aching by 3pm?") or a scroll-stopping demo of the adjustments, not a "buy now." Test angles before details: problem-led vs. proof-led (reviews/ratings) vs. offer-led (free trial / free shipping).
- Test one variable at a time so a result means something, and give each test enough conversions to be real — don't call a winner on 40 clicks.

## What to watch
Judge on **CPA** against the target you set, not on clicks or impressions. If CTR is low, that's a creative/targeting problem. If CTR is good but few buy, that's a page/offer problem, not the ad. Change one lever at a time and give it a few days of data before the next edit — strangling campaigns before they've gathered signal is the most common way to waste money.
