**Why not:** In a breach, the response *becomes* the story. Spin and minimization don't make a breach go away — they create a second, bigger story when the facts come out (and with exposed emails, they will: affected users, security researchers, and journalists can verify what happened). A statement that denies fault you actually bear is also a legal liability. Most breach-notification laws (GDPR, CCPA, and the US state breach statutes) require timely, accurate disclosure; a downplaying statement can turn a contained incident into a regulatory and trust catastrophe. People forgive handled mistakes. They don't forgive cover-ups.

The genuinely smart move is the same one that's honest: get ahead of it, lead with the people affected, own what's yours plainly, and say what you're doing about it. That's what protects the brand. Here's a statement that does that — and it's a *better* PR outcome than a denial.

### Why this version protects you better than a denial

- **It's verifiable, so it survives scrutiny.** Exposed-email breaches get checked. A statement that holds up under verification ends the news cycle; one that's caught minimizing restarts it bigger.
- **It leads with the people affected** (phishing risk, what to watch for), which reads as responsible rather than defensive — the single biggest driver of how a breach response is judged.
- **It owns the failure plainly without over-disclosing.** "We fell short and take responsibility" is accountability; it is not a legal admission of specific negligence. Have Legal review the exact wording, but accountability and legal caution are not in conflict here.
- **It keeps one source of truth.** Pair this with aligned support scripts, internal notice to employees *first or simultaneously*, and a status page, so nothing contradicts.

### Before you publish — checklist

1. **Legal and Security sign-off** on every bracketed fact and on the accountability wording. This is non-negotiable.
2. **Check your notification obligations.** Breach-notification timelines and required content vary by jurisdiction (GDPR's 72-hour authority notice, US state laws, etc.). A lawyer should confirm what you must send, to whom, and by when — this statement does not replace required individual or regulator notifications.
3. **Brief employees first or simultaneously.** Staff hearing it from the news is its own crisis.
4. **Designate one spokesperson** and route all inbound (press, customers, support) through aligned messaging.
5. **Don't delete, don't go dark, don't quietly edit** the statement after the fact — each becomes its own story.
