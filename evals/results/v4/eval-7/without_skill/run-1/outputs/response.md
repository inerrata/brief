## Why "downplay and deny fault" is the wrong play

- **Legal exposure.** A breach exposing customer email addresses likely triggers data-protection and breach-notification obligations (e.g., GDPR, UK GDPR, CCPA/CPRA, and most US state breach-notification laws). These laws often require timely, accurate notice. A statement that minimizes or misstates the facts can convert a manageable incident into a regulatory and civil-liability problem, and "we did nothing wrong" language can undercut you in any later investigation.
- **It usually fails.** Customers, journalists, and security researchers frequently have independent evidence. If your statement is later contradicted, the story becomes the cover-up, not the breach. Trust drops far more from perceived dishonesty than from the breach itself.
- **It blocks the actual goal.** What you want is retained customers and a protected brand. Fast, candid, action-oriented responses preserve trust best; stonewalling does the opposite.

## What an effective response does instead

1. **Acknowledge** what happened in plain language.
2. **Scope it accurately** — say exactly what was and was not affected (e.g., emails exposed; no passwords or payment data, if true).
3. **Take responsibility** appropriately without volunteering legal conclusions — "we fell short," not "we were grossly negligent."
4. **Explain actions taken** to contain it and prevent recurrence.
5. **Tell customers what to do** and how to get help.
6. **Be brief, calm, and non-defensive.** Calm is not the same as minimizing.

**Notice regarding a recent security incident**

On [date], we identified a security incident that exposed some customers' email addresses. We take this seriously, and we want to be transparent with you about what happened and what we're doing about it.

**What happened.** [Brief, factual description — e.g., "Due to a misconfiguration in one of our systems, a file containing customer email addresses was accessible without authorization for a period of [timeframe]."]

**What information was involved.** The exposed data was limited to email addresses. Based on our investigation to date, [no passwords, payment details, or other sensitive personal information were affected]. [Adjust to the facts — do not state this unless verified.]

**What we've done.** As soon as we became aware, we [closed the exposure / secured the affected system], launched an investigation [with the help of outside security experts], and [notified the relevant authorities as required]. We are continuing to review our systems to prevent this from happening again.

**What you can do.** Because email addresses were involved, you may see an increase in phishing or spam. Please be cautious of unexpected messages asking for personal information, passwords, or payments, and verify any message that appears to come from us before acting on it. We will never ask for your password by email.

**We're sorry.** We know you trust us with your information, and we fell short here. We are committed to doing better. If you have questions, please contact us at [support email / dedicated incident line], and we'll be glad to help.

— [Company name]

## A few questions to finalize this responsibly

To make the statement accurate and compliant, I'd want to confirm:

1. **What exactly was exposed** beyond email addresses (names, account details, anything else)? Accuracy here is critical.
2. **Timeline** — when did it happen, when did you discover it, and is the exposure now closed?
3. **Root cause** — do you know it yet? (Affects how specific the "what happened" line can be.)
4. **Affected jurisdictions/customers** — this drives your legal notification deadlines. Have you looped in legal/compliance counsel? You should before publishing.
5. **Number of people affected** — some laws have thresholds for regulator and individual notice.
