## Why I won't help hide it

- **It's illegal.** In the US, the FTC requires that any paid, gifted, or incentivized endorsement be disclosed "clearly and conspicuously." The UK (ASA/CMA) and most other markets have equivalent rules. Liability lands on *you*, the advertiser — not just the creator. The FTC has gone after brands directly, with fines and consent decrees.
- **Supplements are a high-scrutiny category.** Undisclosed endorsements plus health/wellness claims is exactly the combination regulators and the FTC's health-products division look for. You're picking the riskiest possible place to cut this corner.
- **It blows up the asset you're paying for.** When an undisclosed paid post gets exposed — and they do, via screenshots, a disgruntled creator, or a competitor's report — you don't just lose the post. You lose the creator's audience trust (the thing you rented), and the brand wears a "tried to trick us" story. That's far more expensive than a dip in likes.

## Now the real problem: #ad isn't actually what's tanking your engagement

The disclosure tag is usually the scapegoat for an underlying mismatch. Disclosed creator content performs fine all the time — the posts that tank are the ones that *read like ads*, and the tag just confirms what the audience already felt. Here's where to actually look:

**1. You're probably over-scripting it.** The single biggest engagement killer in creator content is brand-controlled copy. The audience followed that creator for *their* voice, and a post written in your marketing voice feels like a commercial regardless of the hashtag. Give the creator the core message and your must-haves (key claim, code, link, disclosure), then let them say it their way. Their authentic take + a disclosure tag beats a polished script every time.

**2. Fit may be off.** If the creator's audience doesn't genuinely overlap your buyer — or the creator wouldn't plausibly use a supplement themselves — the post reads as a cash grab and engagement dies. Vet for real audience match and authentic engagement (comment quality, believable engagement rate for their size), not just follower count.

**3. One-off paid posts underperform relationships.** A creator who's genuinely used the product over weeks and talks about it across several posts is far more credible than a one-shot "here's my sponsor." Build repeat collaborations with a smaller set of well-matched creators.

## What to do instead (this is the version that works)

- **Disclose properly and stop hiding it** — "Paid partnership" label or `#ad` up front, not buried below the fold. Owning it actually *reads as more confident*, not less.
- **Hand creators a brief, not a script.** One core message + must-haves + must-nots, then explicitly invite their own voice and format. This single change usually recovers more engagement than anything else.
- **Tighten fit.** Pick micro-creators whose audience is actually your buyer and who'd plausibly take the product. A pile of well-matched micro-creators beats one mismatched bigger name.
- **Shift to affiliate/code structure for the conversion goal.** Give each creator a unique discount code or tracked link. Codes are inherently disclosed, they let the creator frame it as "a deal for my people" (which audiences *like*), and they tell you which creators actually drive sales — so you stop paying the ones who don't.
- **Lead with a genuine reason to care, not the pitch.** The creator opens on a real problem or moment ("my afternoon energy crash") and the product is the bridge — disclosed, but woven into something the audience would watch anyway.
- **Make disclosure a contractual must-have.** Put it in the brief and the agreement. It protects you legally and it's table stakes for the better-performing creators worth working with.
