## The hard truth: dark mode alone isn't a TechCrunch story

TechCrunch covers funding rounds, genuinely new categories, surprising data, big company moves, and products that change how something works. "We added dark mode to our note-taking app" doesn't clear that bar — it's a feature most apps shipped years ago, in one of the most crowded categories on earth (Notion, Obsidian, Apple Notes, Google Keep, Evernote...). A reporter scanning their inbox for five seconds will see "dark mode" + "note app" and hit delete, and that non-response makes the *next* pitch harder.

Run any pitch through the standard news hooks. A story needs at least one:

- **New or first** — a real launch, a new category, a milestone that matters. Dark mode isn't first; it's table stakes.
- **Data/insight** — proprietary numbers that tell readers something they didn't know.
- **Trend-tied** — your fresh take on a story already in the news.
- **Human/counterintuitive** — an underdog, a reversal, a surprise.

Dark mode fails all four. So before I write a pitch, the real question is **what's actually newsworthy here that dark mode is a symptom of?**

## Where a real story might be hiding

Pick whichever is true — these are the angles worth a journalist's time:

1. **Data angle (the strongest).** You presumably have usage data. "We shipped dark mode and 70% of our users switched within a week" — or data on *when* people write notes (a spike at 11pm-1am?) — is a real insight about how people work. Original data is the single most reliable way to earn coverage. A pitch like *"Data: most note-taking now happens after dark"* is something a reporter can actually run.
2. **The bigger launch this is part of.** Is dark mode one feature in a larger redesign or 2.0? Pitch the redesign, with dark mode as a detail. One feature isn't news; a reimagined product can be.
3. **The traction angle.** If you've hit a real milestone — funding, a user/revenue threshold, notable growth — *that's* the story, and the feature update is color. "Note app crosses X users / raises $Y" gets opened.
4. **A genuine differentiator.** If your app does something the category leaders don't (a novel sync model, local-first privacy, a unique input method), the news is *that*, illustrated by your shipping pace.

If none of those is true yet, dark mode is an **owned-channel** moment, not an earned-media one: ship a changelog post, an in-app announcement, a short social thread, an email to existing users. That's the right home for a feature update, and it's not a consolation prize — it's where these wins actually land with the people who care.

## If you do have an angle — here's the pitch

The pitch email is what wins coverage, not a press release. Keep it ~150 words, lead with the news, and personalize it to a specific reporter who covers productivity/consumer apps (e.g. read their recent pieces; a targeted note to the right 5 reporters beats a blast to 500). Below is a template built on the **data angle** — swap in your real numbers.

> **Subject:** Data: [X]% of note-taking now happens after dark
>
> Hi [First name] — your piece on [their recent article on productivity tools / consumer apps] is why I'm sending this to you specifically.
>
> We just shipped dark mode in [App], and the rollout surfaced something we didn't expect: **[X]% of our [N] users now write notes between [time] and [time]** — note-taking has quietly become a late-night, low-light habit. [One more concrete data point: e.g. dark-mode adoption rate, or how session length changed.]
>
> It's a small window into how and when people actually capture ideas now — might be a fit for your readers thinking about how productivity tools fit into real daily life.
>
> Happy to share the full breakdown, screenshots, or a quick call with our [founder/CEO, name]. Want the data?
>
> [Your name, title, contact]

**Why this works:** the subject leads with an insight, not "Company announces feature." The first line earns the open by being clearly *for this reporter*. The body is data, not promotion — it serves their readers, which is the only reason a journalist runs anything for free. The ask is one easy yes.
