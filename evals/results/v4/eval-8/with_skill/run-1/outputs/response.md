# Influencer campaign plan for your skincare brand

*Assuming: a DTC skincare brand selling online, US-based (so FTC disclosure rules apply), goal = awareness + first-time trial of a hero product, modest starting budget. Adjust any of these and I'll re-tune the plan. A few sharpening questions are at the bottom.*

Skincare is one of the best categories for influencer marketing - people trust faces and routines over brand claims, before/after is native to the format, and the audience actively follows creators for recommendations. Here's how to set it up so it actually converts and doesn't just buy you vanity reach.

---

## 1. Get the strategy right before you spend

**Pick creators on fit, not follower count.** A creator whose audience is your actual buyer - your skin type, your concern (acne, aging, sensitivity, texture), your price point - beats a bigger but mismatched name every time. Relevance converts; raw reach doesn't.

**Go micro and mid-tier, not mega.** Creators in the ~10k-100k range usually have higher engagement, more trust, and far better cost-efficiency. For a new brand, a portfolio of 8-15 well-matched micro-creators will almost always outperform one expensive celebrity post - and it gives you more content, more data, and less single-point risk.

**One core message per campaign.** Decide the single thing every creator should land. For skincare that's usually *one hero product solving one specific problem* ("cleared my texture in 4 weeks," "the only SPF that doesn't pill under makeup"). Don't ask creators to cover your whole range - pick the wedge product.

---

## 2. Find and vet creators (do this before paying anyone)

**Where to source:**
- Search your category hashtags (#skincareroutine, #acnejourney, #sensitiveskin, plus your specific concern).
- See who your existing customers and followers already follow.
- Look at who's posting about competitor products - their audience is pre-qualified.
- Skin-type and concern communities (e.g. rosacea, hyperpigmentation, "skinimalism") punch above their size.

**Vet every creator for authenticity before money moves** - follower counts are gameable:

| Check | What good looks like | Red flag |
|---|---|---|
| Engagement rate | Believable for their size (micro often 3-8%) | High followers, near-zero engagement = bought audience |
| Comment quality | Real conversation, skin questions, "where can I buy" | Generic emoji/bot spam |
| Audience match | Their followers are your buyers - ask for audience demographics screenshots | Wrong geo, wrong age, wrong skin profile |
| Growth curve | Steady organic climb | Overnight follower spikes |
| Brand safety | Past content and other partnerships fit your values | Controversies, conflicting promos, off-brand context |

Ask for results from comparable campaigns - but verify, since screenshots are easy to fake.

**One category-specific caution:** skincare makes health/efficacy claims that can cross into regulated territory. Avoid creators who'll promise to "cure" conditions or make medical claims, and don't put those claims in your brief - keep it to cosmetic, experiential language ("my skin felt smoother," not "treats eczema") unless you have clinical substantiation.

---

## 3. The brief - direction without a script

The single biggest mistake brands make is over-scripting. The creator knows their audience better than you do; over-controlled content reads as an ad and tanks. Give them the message and the must-haves, then get out of the way.

**Your creator brief should include:**

- **One core message + the takeaway** you want their audience to walk away with.
- **Must-haves:** the hero product, the offer/code, the link, the disclosure tag, and 1-2 key claims you *can* substantiate.
- **Must-nots:** no medical/curative claims, no competitor disparagement, no false "I've used this for years" if they haven't, no off-brand context.
- **What you are NOT dictating:** explicitly invite their authentic voice, format, and honest opinion - including showing real results over time, not a one-take rave.
- **Logistics:** deliverables (e.g. 1 in-feed Reel + 3 stories), timing, and usage rights in writing if you'll repurpose the content into paid ads.

**A skincare-native content angle that works:** the multi-week "routine diary" or "X-day update" beats a single post - it shows real use, matches how people actually evaluate skincare, and gives the creator something honest to say. Build that into the deliverable where you can.

---

## 4. Deal structure

- **Gifted product** for smaller creators (common and fine in skincare - just disclose it).
- **Flat fee** for mid-tier with proven performance.
- **Hybrid (fee + affiliate code/commission)** is ideal: it aligns incentives and lets you measure conversions per creator.
- **Tie part of payment to deliverables, not just promises.**
- For any larger spend, **start with a paid test post before committing to a long-term deal.**

Relationships beat one-offs: a creator who genuinely likes the product and keeps showing up over several collaborations is far more credible - and cheaper - than a rotating cast of one-shot posts. Treat your best performers as ongoing partners.

---

## 5. Disclosure - non-negotiable, and you're liable too

Every paid, gifted, or commissioned post **must be clearly and conspicuously disclosed** - it's the law (FTC in the US, ASA in the UK, equivalents elsewhere), and it's the basis of the trust you're borrowing.

- Require #ad / "Paid partnership" / clear affiliate-link disclosure on **every** placement - including gifted product, which counts as compensation.
- Disclosure must be visible up front, not buried below a "more" fold or in a wall of hashtags.
- Never script a creator to claim they bought or use a product they didn't, hide the relationship, or pose an ad as organic opinion.
- **Make disclosure a contractual must-have** in every agreement - regulators can hold the brand liable, not just the creator.

Disclosed partnerships convert perfectly well. There's no upside to hiding it and real legal/reputational downside.

---

## 6. Measure it against the right goal

Awareness plays need awareness metrics - don't force a last-click sales number onto a top-funnel post and call it a failure.

- **Per-creator tracking:** give every creator a **unique discount code and/or tracked link** so you know which relationships actually drive results.
- **Awareness signals:** reach, engagement rate, saves/shares, referral traffic, and **branded-search lift** (are more people Googling your brand after the posts?).
- **Direct-response signals:** code redemptions, conversion rate, and CPA/ROAS per creator against a target.
- **Watch incrementality:** some code-driven "sales" would have happened anyway (especially if the code leaks to deal sites). Measure lift, not just attributed revenue, before scaling spend.
- **Judge creators over time,** not on a single post - the compounding value of someone who keeps showing up is the real return.

---

## Suggested starting setup (a concrete first move)

1. Pick **one hero product** and one core message.
2. Build a list of **15-20 candidate micro/mid creators** matched on skin concern + audience demographics.
3. **Vet down to 8-12** using the table above.
4. Offer **gifted product + affiliate code** (hybrid fee for the strongest 2-3).
5. Send the **brief** with must-haves, must-nots, and contractual disclosure.
6. Assign **unique codes/links**, run for ~4-6 weeks, and review per-creator performance.
7. **Re-sign your top 2-3 performers** into an ongoing relationship; cut the rest.
