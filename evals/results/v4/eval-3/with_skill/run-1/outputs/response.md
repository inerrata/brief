Good instinct to ask "where do I begin" before throwing money at it — most people start by picking a channel and burning budget. The order below front-loads the decisions that actually determine whether ads work.

Assuming: B2B SaaS, self-serve $49/mo subscription, you're the one running this (small budget, no agency); adjust if wrong.

## The one rule that matters most

Paid ads amplify what you already have. They don't fix a weak offer or a page that doesn't convert — they just help you lose money faster. So before you spend a dollar, get these four things in place.

## Step 1 — Know what a customer is worth (do the math first)

You can't tell if ads are working without a target cost-per-acquisition (CPA). Quick version for a $49/mo plan:

- **Figure out average customer lifetime.** If a customer stays ~12 months, lifetime revenue ≈ $588 (before churn/discounts). If they stay ~20 months, ~$980.
- **Apply a margin.** SaaS gross margin is often ~80%, so lifetime *gross profit* on a 12-month customer ≈ $470.
- **Set your CAC ceiling.** A common target is 3:1 LTV:CAC, which on that example means you can spend up to ~$155 to acquire a customer and still have healthy economics. (Your real numbers may differ — plug in your actual churn and margin.)

That ceiling is your scoreboard. If ads bring customers in under it, scale. If not, fix or stop. **Without this number you're flying blind**, so don't skip it.

One nuance for subscriptions: you pay CAC upfront but earn revenue monthly, so watch your **payback period** (how many months of $49 it takes to recoup acquisition cost) alongside the ratio — it affects how fast you can afford to spend.

## Step 2 — Make sure the page converts and you can track it

- **Conversion tracking installed and tested before launch.** Google Ads tag and/or Meta Pixel, firing on the real conversion event (trial start or paid signup, not just a pageview). Test it end to end. If you can't attribute a signup to a campaign, you'll never know what's working.
- **The landing page does one job.** Sending paid clicks to a page that converts at 2% vs. 4% doubles your effective CPA before you touch the ad account. The page should match the ad's promise and drive a single action (start trial / start free).

## Step 3 — Pick ONE channel, matched to how buyers find you

Don't run four channels on a starter budget — you'll under-power all of them and learn nothing. The choice comes down to whether demand already exists:

- **Are people already searching for "project management software," "[competitor] alternative," "task management tool"?** Yes — they almost certainly are. **Start with Google Search.** It captures existing demand: someone is already looking for what you sell, and you pay to be the answer. Highest intent, fastest signal, easiest to read. For a known-category product like PM software, this is where I'd start.
- **Paid social (Meta, LinkedIn, Reddit)** *creates* demand by interrupting people who weren't looking. Useful later, and LinkedIn can be precise for B2B (but expensive). It's a worse *first* channel for a category buyers already search for — save it for once search is working.

My recommendation: **start with Google Search**, capturing high-intent category and competitor-alternative queries. Get it profitable, then expand to retargeting and social.

## Step 4 — Structure a small, readable account

- **A few tight ad groups by keyword theme** — e.g. "project management software," "[competitor] alternatives," "task tracking for teams" — so the ad matches the query. Don't over-segment; a small budget split 20 ways never gathers enough data to optimize.
- **Use automated bidding** (Max Conversions, or Target CPA once you have ~15–30 conversions of history) and feed it your tested conversion signal.
- **Budget enough to learn.** Better to fund one channel properly than starve three. A few hundred dollars a month is enough to start *reading signal* on search; scale only once CPA looks right.

## Step 5 — Match the ask to how warm they are

- **Cold search traffic** is mid-intent — they're problem-aware and shopping. A free trial or "start free" is the right low-friction ask. Don't demand a demo call or a credit card up front if you can avoid it.
- **Add retargeting next**, not first. People who visited pricing or started a trial and didn't convert are your cheapest wins — a direct "finish setting up / start your trial" message closes them. Build this once you have site traffic to retarget.

## Step 6 — Launch, then read before you fiddle

- **Test message angles before details.** Try a problem-led ad ("Stop chasing status updates in Slack") vs. a proof-led one vs. an offer-led one. Find the angle that resonates before optimizing headlines or button color.
- **Judge on CPA, not clicks.** CTR and impressions are vanity. Your scoreboard is cost per trial/signup vs. the ceiling from Step 1. Diagnose down the chain: low CTR = creative/targeting; good CTR but no signups = page/offer mismatch.
- **Don't strangle it.** Give campaigns days of data before editing, and change one lever at a time. Killing campaigns before they exit the learning phase is the #1 rookie mistake.

## The honest first move

If I had to compress this to a single first action: **work out your CAC ceiling (Step 1) and confirm your trial-signup tracking fires correctly (Step 2).** Those two gate everything else, and most failed ad spends trace back to skipping them. Then launch a small Google Search campaign on your category and competitor-alternative keywords.
