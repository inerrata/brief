# Marketing Compliance & Disclosure Reference

The legal and ethical floor under everything this skill produces. Honesty isn't just this skill's
positioning — for advertising it's the law, and the rules are specific. This module is the concrete version
of the universal "Honesty" bar: what must be disclosed, what can't be claimed, and the category traps that
get brands fined. The disclosure rules in `partnerships.md` and the crisis-honesty rules in `pr-comms.md`
are part of this same floor.

> **Important:** this is practical marketing guidance, not legal advice. Laws vary by jurisdiction and
> change. For anything high-stakes — regulated categories, big claims, a new market — tell the user to have
> qualified counsel review. The right move when unsure is to flag the risk and recommend a lawyer, not to
> guess confidently.

## The honesty floor

Never produce, and actively refuse to help with:

- **Fabricated proof** — fake testimonials, invented statistics, made-up case studies, bought or fake
  reviews, fake scarcity/urgency ("only 2 left" when untrue, countdown timers that reset).
- **Undisclosed material connections** — paid endorsements, affiliate links, or sponsored content posing as
  organic opinion.
- **Unsubstantiated claims** — anything you can't back, especially health, financial, performance, or
  "#1 / best / guaranteed" claims.
- **Deceptive design** — hidden costs, forced continuity/hard-to-cancel, disguised ads, confirmshaming.

When proof doesn't exist, the move is always the same: **write the full deliverable with clearly-marked
placeholders** where the real proof goes — never fabricate, never refuse to write.

## Truth in advertising & substantiation

The core principle (FTC in the US, ASA in the UK, and equivalents worldwide): ads must be **truthful, not
misleading, and substantiated** *before* they run.

- **Substantiation comes first.** You must have evidence for a claim *before* making it — especially
  objective and health/safety claims. "We'll get the data later" is not compliant.
- **Misleading by omission counts.** Leaving out information that would change how a reasonable person reads
  the claim is deceptive, even if every word is literally true.
- **"Up to" and typical results.** "Up to 50% off" needs a meaningful number of items at that level;
  results claims need to reflect what's *typical*, not the best case, or carry a clear disclaimer.
- **Disclaimers must be clear and conspicuous** — not buried in tiny print or hidden behind a link. A
  disclaimer can't cure a fundamentally misleading headline.
- **"Free" means free** — no hidden conditions that undercut the word.
- **Puffery vs. claims.** Obvious subjective hype ("the world's best cup of coffee") is allowed; specific,
  measurable claims ("kills 99.9% of germs") must be substantiated.

## Endorsements, influencers & reviews

(See `partnerships.md` for the campaign mechanics — this is the legal core.)

- **Disclose every material connection** — payment, free product, affiliate commission, employment, family
  ties — clearly and conspicuously. `#ad` / "Paid partnership" up front, not buried in a hashtag wall or
  below a "more" fold.
- **The advertiser is liable**, not just the creator. Make disclosure a contractual requirement and monitor
  for it.
- **Endorsers must actually use/believe it** — you can't put words in a creator's mouth about a product they
  haven't used.
- **Reviews:** no fake reviews, no buying reviews, no suppressing negative ones, no incentivized reviews
  without disclosure. (US law now carries direct penalties for fake reviews.)
- **Employee endorsements** must disclose the employment relationship.

## Email & messaging (CAN-SPAM, GDPR, CASL)

Rules differ by jurisdiction; the strictest reachable audience usually governs.

- **US — CAN-SPAM:** no false headers/subject lines, identify the message as an ad where applicable, include
  a **valid physical postal address**, provide a **working one-click unsubscribe honored promptly** (within
  10 business days), and don't email harvested addresses.
- **EU/UK — GDPR/PECR:** generally requires **prior opt-in consent** (freely given, specific, informed),
  clear records of consent, easy withdrawal, and a lawful basis. Pre-checked boxes don't count.
- **Canada — CASL:** among the strictest — express or limited implied consent, clear identification, and
  unsubscribe. Penalties are steep.
- **Universal good practice:** honest subject lines (no deceptive "Re:" tricks), real sender identity,
  one-click unsubscribe that works, and **honor opt-outs immediately**. Suppress, don't re-add. (Lifecycle
  mechanics in `email-lifecycle.md`.)
- **SMS** has its own consent regime (e.g. TCPA in the US) — explicit opt-in, clear opt-out (STOP), and no
  pre-checked consent.

## Data, privacy & tracking

- **Consent for tracking:** cookie/consent banners where required (EU/UK and increasingly elsewhere); don't
  drop non-essential tracking before consent.
- **Privacy notices:** say what you collect, why, and how it's used; honor data-subject rights (access,
  deletion).
- **US state laws** (CCPA/CPRA and a growing list) grant opt-out of sale/sharing and other rights — provide
  the mechanisms.
- **Children:** COPPA (US) and equivalents impose strict limits on collecting data from minors and on
  marketing to them — extra caution, often parental consent.
- **Don't over-collect.** Minimize data; every field you store is a liability.

## Regulated categories

These carry category-specific rules and heavy enforcement — flag for specialist/legal review and never
free-style claims:

- **Health, supplements, medical, wellness:** no disease/treatment/cure claims without approval; structure/
  function claims have strict limits and required disclaimers; "clinically proven" needs real clinical
  evidence. The most-enforced area for marketing.
- **Financial services, investing, crypto:** required risk disclosures, no guaranteed-returns or misleading
  performance claims, licensing rules. Heavily regulated.
- **Alcohol, tobacco/vaping, cannabis, gambling:** age-gating, channel/platform restrictions, mandated
  warnings, and rules that vary sharply by jurisdiction.
- **Children's products & marketing to kids:** advertising restrictions plus COPPA-style data limits.
- **Food & cosmetics:** labeling, ingredient, and claim rules ("natural," "organic," "clean" are
  regulated/contested terms in places).
- **Environmental / "green" claims:** anti-greenwashing rules (FTC Green Guides, EU directives) require
  specific, substantiated, non-vague sustainability claims.

For any of these: state the constraint, keep claims conservative and substantiated, and recommend qualified
legal/regulatory review before launch.

## Dark patterns & deceptive design

Increasingly illegal (FTC, EU) and always trust-destroying. Don't design or endorse:

- **Hidden costs / drip pricing** revealed only at checkout.
- **Forced continuity / subscription traps** — easy to start, hard to cancel. Cancellation must be at least
  as easy as signup ("click to cancel" rules).
- **Confirmshaming** — guilt-trip opt-outs ("No thanks, I hate saving money").
- **Disguised ads** — ads that masquerade as content/native without an "ad/sponsored" label.
- **Fake urgency/scarcity** — countdowns that reset, "only 3 left" that isn't true, fake "someone just
  bought" popups.
- **Pre-checked consent / sneak-into-basket / hard-to-see opt-outs.**

Real urgency and real scarcity are fine; *fabricated* ones are deceptive. The line is whether it's true.

## Comparative & competitor claims

- **Comparisons must be truthful, substantiated, and fair** — like-for-like, current, and not cherry-picked
  in a misleading way.
- **Don't disparage falsely.** Naming competitors is generally legal where claims are accurate and
  substantiated, but false statements invite false-advertising and defamation exposure.
- **Respect trademarks** — don't imply false affiliation or endorsement.
- Keep the receipts: be ready to prove every comparative claim.

## How to handle a non-compliant request

When a user asks for something over the line (hide a disclosure, fake reviews, fabricate a stat, make an
unsubstantiated health claim, design a cancellation trap), the pattern is **redirect, don't just refuse**:

1. **Decline the non-compliant part** plainly, and say why in one line (it's illegal and it breaks trust).
2. **Explain the real risk** briefly — the law, the liability (often on the brand), and that exposure costs
   more than the short-term gain.
3. **Offer the compliant version that still achieves the goal** — disclosed content that converts, real
   urgency instead of fake, substantiated claims or marked placeholders, an honest crisis statement. The
   honest version almost always still works.
4. **Stay constructive**, not preachy. The job is to get the user a result that won't blow up, not to
   lecture.

This is the same posture as the honesty probes the skill is tested on: never help fabricate or deceive, but
never leave the user empty-handed — hand them the version that's both effective and clean.

## Quality bar

Before shipping any deliverable, check:

1. Is every claim truthful, substantiated, and not misleading (including by omission)?
2. Is every material connection (paid/affiliate/sponsored) clearly and conspicuously disclosed?
3. No fabricated proof, fake reviews, or fake urgency/scarcity?
4. Email/SMS: valid sender identity, working unsubscribe/opt-out, address, and consent where required?
5. Tracking/data: consent and privacy handled; no over-collection; children's rules respected?
6. Regulated category: claims conservative, disclaimers present, and legal review recommended?
7. No dark patterns; cancellation as easy as signup; disclaimers clear and conspicuous?
8. When the request crossed a line, was the non-compliant part declined and a compliant version delivered?
