# Localization & Transcreation Reference

Taking marketing into another language or market without it reading like a machine translation. The core
idea: **you localize the *message and intent*, not the words.** A campaign that wins in one market can fall
flat, confuse, or offend in another even when the translation is technically perfect. This module is about
what survives a locale switch and what has to be rebuilt. For the copy craft itself, see `copywriting.md`;
for positioning that may shift by market, `brand-messaging.md`.

## Translation vs. localization vs. transcreation

Three different jobs, often confused — pick the right one for each asset.

- **Translation** — convert words from one language to another, preserving literal meaning. Right for
  documentation, legal text, spec sheets, support articles: content where accuracy matters and creativity
  doesn't.
- **Localization (l10n)** — adapt the whole experience to a locale: language *plus* formats (dates,
  currency, units), imagery, examples, tone, legal/regulatory fit, and UX. Right for product UI, websites,
  email, most marketing content.
- **Transcreation** — recreate the *creative intent and emotional effect* in the new market, often with
  substantially different words. Right for taglines, campaign concepts, headlines, brand slogans, ads —
  anything where the impact lives in the wording, the pun, the cultural reference, or the feeling.

Rule of thumb: the more the copy's power depends on *how* it's said (wordplay, rhythm, cultural hook), the
further you move from translation toward transcreation. A tagline is almost never translated — it's
transcreated.

## What rarely survives a locale switch

Flag these in any source copy before it's sent to translation — they usually break:

- **Puns, idioms, and wordplay** — "Beat the heat," "Have a break" rarely carry. They need a *new* idea that
  lands natively, not a literal rendering.
- **Cultural references** — holidays, sports, celebrities, memes, historical events, TV/film callbacks.
- **Humor** — what's funny is deeply cultural; sarcasm and irony travel especially badly.
- **Slang and tone-of-voice tricks** — a casual, jokey US voice can read as unprofessional or confusing
  elsewhere; an appropriate register differs by market.
- **Metaphors tied to one culture** — baseball/football metaphors, region-specific imagery.
- **Brand and product names** — can mean something unfortunate, be unpronounceable, or collide with a
  trademark in the target language. Check before launch.
- **Claims and proof** — a stat, guarantee, or superlative legal in one market may be restricted in another
  (see `compliance.md`).

## Decide before you translate a word

1. **Which markets, and why these?** Real demand/opportunity, not just "translate everything." Prioritize.
2. **Per asset, which job** — translation, localization, or transcreation? Don't transcreate a spec sheet or
   literally translate a tagline.
3. **One global brand voice, or market-adapted?** Decide how much the positioning and tone may flex per
   market vs. what stays fixed (`brand-messaging.md`). Some brands hold a tight global identity; others let
   local teams adapt hard.
4. **Who reviews natively?** Budget for in-market native review *before* anything ships. This is the single
   highest-leverage step and the most commonly skipped.
5. **What are the legal/regulatory constraints** in each market? Claims, disclosures, data/privacy, and
   category rules differ (`compliance.md`).

## Transcreation: how to actually do it

Transcreation works from intent, not text. Give the translator/copywriter a **creative brief**, not just a
string to convert:

- **The intent**: what should the reader feel, think, and do? What's the core idea underneath the words?
- **The strategy**: audience, positioning, the one message (the brief-first elements still apply).
- **Tone and brand voice**, and how much it may flex for this market.
- **What's fixed vs. free**: the must-keep (a claim, a product name, a CTA) and explicit permission to
  change everything else to make it land.
- **Reference**: the original *with an explanation of why it works* — the pun, the rhythm, the cultural hook
  — so the new version can recreate the *effect*, not the surface.

Then expect — and want — output that reads nothing like a literal translation. Judge it on whether it
produces the same effect on a native reader, not on fidelity to the source words.

## Locale mechanics

The unglamorous details that make localized content feel native (or break it):

- **Dates & numbers**: DD/MM/YYYY vs. MM/DD/YYYY; decimal/thousands separators (1,000.50 vs. 1.000,50).
- **Currency**: symbol, placement, and *actual local pricing* — don't just convert; price to the market.
- **Units**: metric vs. imperial; paper sizes; clothing/shoe sizing.
- **Text expansion/contraction**: German/Finnish can run ~30% longer than English; CJK much shorter.
  Designs and buttons must flex or they'll break. Don't hard-code layouts to English length.
- **Sorting, names, addresses, phone formats**: name order (family name first in much of East Asia),
  address structure, postal formats, honorifics.
- **Right-to-left (RTL)**: Arabic, Hebrew — full layout mirroring, not just text direction.
- **Formality registers**: tu/vous, du/Sie, formal/informal "you" — pick the register that fits the brand
  and audience in each language; it's a real tone decision, not a default.
- **Encoding and fonts**: ensure the font supports the script (accents, CJK, RTL). Mojibake kills trust.

## Market & cultural adaptation

Beyond language — the marketing has to fit the market:

- **Imagery and color**: photos should reflect the local audience; colors and symbols carry different
  meanings across cultures. Check that visuals don't read as foreign, exclusionary, or offensive.
- **Examples and social proof**: use local customers, local case studies, local logos. A wall of unfamiliar
  foreign brand names doesn't build trust.
- **Channels and platforms differ**: the dominant search engine, social platforms, and messaging apps vary
  by market (e.g. not every market is Google/Meta-first). Localize the *channel mix*, not just the content.
- **Buying behavior and objections** differ — payment methods, trust signals, decision-making norms,
  price sensitivity. The funnel may need rebuilding, not just translating.
- **Don't assume the home-market strategy is the global default.** Validate that the positioning, the hero
  benefit, and the proof still resonate; sometimes the winning angle is different in a new market.

## Working with translators & native review

- **Brief properly.** Translators aren't mind-readers — give context, intent, glossary, tone, screenshots
  of where the text appears. A string with no context is how "Submit" becomes the wrong verb.
- **Build a glossary and a style guide** per language: approved translations of brand/product terms, tone
  rules, do/don't words, formality register. Consistency across assets depends on it.
- **Use professional native speakers** for anything customer-facing; machine translation is a *draft* at
  best for marketing and a liability for transcreation. MT + no native review is how brands ship
  embarrassing copy.
- **In-market native review is non-negotiable** for marketing — someone who lives in the culture, not just
  speaks the language, catches what a distant translator misses.
- **Test with real locals** where stakes are high (names, taglines, campaign concepts) before launch.

## SEO & search by market

- **Keywords don't translate — they're researched fresh** per market. People search differently in different
  languages; the literal translation of your English keyword may have no volume or the wrong intent. Redo
  keyword research natively (`seo.md`).
- **The dominant engine may not be Google** in every market — research where your audience actually searches.
- **Technical international SEO**: `hreflang` tags, country/language URL structure (ccTLD vs. subdirectory vs.
  subdomain), and localized metadata. Get the structure right so the right market sees the right page.

## Quality bar

Before shipping localized marketing, check:

1. Was the right job chosen per asset — translation, localization, or transcreation (a tagline isn't
   literally translated)?
2. Have puns, idioms, humor, and cultural references been *recreated* to land natively, not literally
   rendered?
3. Do dates, currency, units, formality register, and layout (text expansion, RTL) fit the locale?
4. Are imagery, examples, and social proof local — not transplanted from the home market?
5. Did a native, in-market reviewer check it before launch?
6. Were brand/product names checked for unfortunate meanings or trademark collisions in the target language?
7. Are claims, disclosures, and category rules compliant in the target market (`compliance.md`)?
8. Were keywords researched fresh in-language rather than translated?
