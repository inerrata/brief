# Partnerships & Influencer Reference

Growing through other people's audiences: influencer and creator marketing, affiliates, and co-marketing
partnerships. The common thread is **borrowed trust** — you're renting a relationship someone else built, so
the rules are about fit, authenticity, and fair value exchange. For the paid mechanics of affiliate/ad spend,
see `paid-media.md`; for disclosure-heavy compliance, the honesty rules below are load-bearing.

## The three types

- **Influencer/creator** — pay (or gift) a creator to make content for *their* audience. You borrow their
  trust and reach. Best for awareness and consideration in audiences that follow people, not brands.
- **Affiliate** — pay partners a commission for conversions they drive, via tracked links/codes.
  Pay-for-performance, lower risk, scales with many small partners. Best for direct response.
- **Co-marketing** — two brands with overlapping-but-not-competing audiences create value together (a joint
  webinar, guide, bundle, integration). No money changes hands; you trade audiences. Best for B2B and reach
  into an adjacent market.

Pick by goal: awareness → influencer; direct conversions → affiliate; credibility + reach into an adjacent
audience → co-marketing.

## Influencer & creator marketing

- **Fit beats follower count.** A creator whose audience genuinely overlaps your buyer, who'd plausibly use the
  product, outperforms a bigger but mismatched name every time. Relevance and trust convert; raw reach doesn't.
- **Micro and mid-tier often beat mega.** Smaller creators (roughly 10k–100k) usually have higher engagement,
  more trust, and better cost-efficiency than celebrities. A pile of well-matched micro-creators frequently
  beats one expensive mega-influencer.
- **Engagement and audience quality > follower count.** Check real engagement, comment quality, and audience
  authenticity — follower counts are gameable (see vetting).
- **Creative freedom converts.** The creator knows their audience better than you do. Give a clear brief on
  the *message and must-haves*, then let them say it in their own voice. Over-scripted, brand-controlled content
  reads as an ad and tanks — the whole point was their authentic voice.
- **Relationships > one-offs.** A creator who uses and likes the product over time, in repeated collaborations,
  is far more credible than a one-shot paid post.

## Finding & vetting creators

- **Source:** search your space's hashtags/topics, see who your customers already follow, check who's covering
  competitors, use platforms/agencies for scale.
- **Vet for authenticity** before paying:
  - **Engagement rate** in a believable range for their size — suspiciously low engagement on high followers =
    bought audience.
  - **Comment quality** — real conversation vs. generic emoji/bot spam.
  - **Audience match** — their followers are actually your buyers (ask for audience demographics/screenshots).
  - **Follower growth pattern** — organic curve vs. overnight spikes (bought followers).
  - **Brand-safety** — past content, controversies, and whether their other partnerships fit your values.
- **Past performance** — ask for results from comparable campaigns, but verify; screenshots are easy to fake.

## The brief & the deal

**A good creator brief gives direction without a script:**
- The **one core message** and the audience takeaway.
- **Must-haves** (key claim, offer/code, disclosure requirement, link) and **must-nots** (false claims,
  competitor mentions, off-brand context).
- **What you're NOT dictating** — explicitly invite their authentic voice and format.
- Deliverables, timing, usage rights, and the disclosure requirement (below) stated up front.

**Deal structures:** flat fee, gifted product (smaller creators), affiliate/commission, or hybrid (fee +
performance). Tie part of the payment to deliverables, not just promises. For a major spend, start with a paid
test post before committing to a long deal. Get usage rights in writing if you'll repurpose the content into
ads (`paid-media.md`).

## Affiliate programs

- **Pay for outcomes, not clicks.** Commission on a sale (or a qualified lead) aligns incentives and caps risk.
- **Recruit partners whose audience matches** — content sites, reviewers, newsletter authors, complementary
  tools. A few high-fit affiliates beat a directory of irrelevant ones.
- **Make it easy to promote:** tracked links/codes, ready creative, clear terms, reliable attribution and
  payouts. Affiliates promote what's easy and what pays reliably.
- **Set commission against your economics** — it's a CAC; size it against LTV like any acquisition cost
  (`measurement.md`).
- **Police quality.** Watch for affiliates using brand-bidding you've prohibited, spammy tactics, coupon-site
  leakage that just discounts customers you'd have won anyway, or fraud. Affiliate revenue can be partly
  non-incremental — measure the lift, not just the attributed sales.

## Co-marketing partnerships

- **Pick complementary, not competing, partners** who serve a similar buyer with a different product (e.g. two
  tools the same team uses). Overlapping audience, non-overlapping offer.
- **Create genuine joint value:** a co-authored guide, a joint webinar, a bundle, an integration, a shared
  event. Both audiences should get something worth their attention.
- **Make the exchange fair and clear** up front — who produces what, how each promotes, how leads/credit are
  shared. Lopsided effort kills the relationship.
- **Integrations are the most durable B2B partnership** — a real product connection gives both sides an ongoing
  reason to co-promote and refer.

## Disclosure & honesty (non-negotiable)

This is the load-bearing rule of the whole module. Paid and incentivized endorsements **must be disclosed** —
it's the law (FTC in the US, ASA in the UK, and equivalents), and it's the basis of the trust you're renting.

- **Every paid, gifted, or commissioned endorsement is disclosed**, clearly and conspicuously — `#ad`,
  `#sponsored`, "paid partnership" labels, affiliate-link disclosure. Buried, vague, or below-the-fold
  disclosure doesn't count.
- **No fake authenticity.** Never script a creator to claim they bought or use a product they didn't, hide a
  paid relationship, or pose ads as organic opinion. Astroturfing and undisclosed endorsements are exactly what
  this skill refuses to help fabricate.
- **No invented results or fake reviews.** Don't manufacture testimonials, buy reviews, or claim creator
  results you can't verify.
- **Make disclosure a contractual must-have** in every brief — you're liable too, not just the creator.

If asked to plan something that hides a paid relationship or fakes authenticity, decline that part and offer
the honest, disclosed version that still works — disclosed partnerships convert fine, and they don't blow up.

## Measuring partnerships

- **Track per-partner** with unique codes/links so you know which relationships actually drive results.
- **Awareness plays** (influencer reach) need awareness metrics (reach, engagement, branded-search lift,
  referral traffic) — don't force a last-click sales number onto a top-funnel post and call it a failure.
- **Direct-response plays** (affiliate, discount codes) measure on CPA/ROAS against your target, like any
  acquisition channel (`measurement.md`).
- **Watch incrementality.** Affiliate and coupon-driven "sales" include people who'd have bought anyway —
  measure the lift, not just attributed revenue, before scaling spend.
- **Judge relationships over time**, not single posts — the compounding value of a creator who keeps showing up
  is the real return.

## Quality bar

Before launching or advising on a partnership, check:

1. Is the partner matched on *audience fit and trust*, not just follower count or reach?
2. Has the creator/affiliate been vetted for authentic, real engagement (not bought audience)?
3. Does the brief give a clear core message and must-haves while leaving authentic voice intact?
4. **Is disclosure required, clear, and contractual on every paid/gifted/commissioned placement?**
5. Is the deal/commission sized against real unit economics?
6. Is the measurement matched to the goal (awareness vs. direct response), with incrementality considered?
7. Nothing fabricated — no fake authenticity, scripted false claims, invented results, or bought reviews?
