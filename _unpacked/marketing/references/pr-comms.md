# PR & Communications Reference

Earned media and public communication: press releases, media pitches, announcements, executive messaging, and
crisis response. The discipline is different from marketing — you're not buying or owning the channel, you're
persuading a journalist, a community, or the public, and you don't control the final wording. For product
*announcements* you control (blog, email, in-app), see `product-marketing.md`; for the launch plan around a
PR moment, `campaigns.md`.

## The core difference from marketing

- **You don't control the message — you influence it.** A journalist rewrites, cuts, and reframes. Your job is
  to make the *true, interesting* version so easy to tell that it survives editing.
- **The journalist is your audience, not the end reader.** Pitch what serves *their* readers and their beat,
  not what serves you. A pitch that's all about your company is an ad they won't run for free.
- **Earned credibility is the whole point.** Third-party coverage carries trust an ad never can — which is
  exactly why it can't be bought or faked. Fabricated quotes, fake "data studies," or planted reviews destroy
  the one thing PR exists to build.
- **Relationships compound, transactions don't.** PR is a long game of being a reliable, honest source.

## Is it actually news?

Most "news" pitches aren't. Before pitching, pass the story through a real news hook:

- **Genuinely new or first** — a launch, a category, a milestone that actually matters (not "we updated our UI").
- **Data/insight** — proprietary numbers or a finding that tells the journalist's audience something they
  didn't know. Original data is the single most reliable earned-media hook.
- **Trend-tied / newsjacking** — your angle on a story already in the news, offered fast while it's hot.
- **Human / counterintuitive** — a surprising story, an underdog, a reversal.
- **Local/relevant** — matters to a specific community or beat.

If it fails all of these, it's not a press story — it's a blog post or a social post. Don't burn a journalist
relationship pitching a non-story; redirect it to owned channels (`product-marketing.md`, `social-media.md`).

## The media pitch

The pitch is the deliverable that does the work — the press release is secondary.

**Structure (short email, ~150 words):**
1. **Subject line** — specific and newsworthy, written for the journalist's inbox. The story in one line, not
   "Press Release: Company Announces…". This is the whole ballgame; most pitches die here.
2. **First line** — why *this journalist* (reference their beat or a recent piece, genuinely) and the hook.
   Generic blasts to 500 reporters are why most pitches are ignored.
3. **The story** — 2–3 sentences: what's new, why it matters, why now. Lead with the news, not the company history.
4. **Why their readers care** — the relevance to *their* audience, stated plainly.
5. **The offer** — what you can give: exclusive, interview, data, demo, assets. Make saying yes easy.
6. **One-line CTA** — "Want the full release / an interview / the data under embargo?"

**Pitch rules:**
- **Personalize or don't send.** A real reason this reporter, on this beat, is the difference between coverage
  and the trash folder.
- **Lead with the news, not the brand.** "[Company] is excited to announce" is an instant delete.
- **Make the journalist's job easy** — offer quotes, data, images, an exclusive. You're reducing their work.
- **Respect the embargo and the exclusive.** Offering the same "exclusive" to five outlets ends relationships.
- **Brevity.** A busy reporter scans for five seconds. If the hook isn't obvious, it's gone.

## Press releases

Still useful as the **record** and the assets bundle — but it's rarely what wins coverage; the pitch is.

**Standard structure:**
- **Headline** — the news, specific and active. Not clever; clear.
- **Dateline + lead paragraph** — who/what/when/where/why in the first sentence. Assume only the headline and
  first line get read.
- **Body** — supporting detail, the most important first (inverted pyramid). Context, the "why now."
- **Quotes** — from a real, named executive (and ideally a customer/partner). Make them say something a person
  would actually say, not corporate boilerplate. A quote that could come from any company adds nothing.
- **Boilerplate** — the standard one-paragraph company description at the end.
- **Contact** — a real human, reachable now.

**Rules:** every fact verifiable, every stat sourced, every quote actually approved by the person. No hype
adjectives a journalist will cut anyway ("revolutionary," "game-changing," "world-class"). If a claim can't be
backed, it doesn't go in — a journalist who catches one fabrication never trusts the source again.

## Building media relationships

- **Build the list before you need it.** Know the specific reporters who cover your space and what they
  actually write. A targeted list of 15 right journalists beats a blast to 500.
- **Engage before you pitch.** Read their work, share it, comment thoughtfully. Be a known, useful name before
  you ask for something.
- **Be a reliable source even when it's not your story** — offer expert commentary, data, or a referral.
  Reporters remember sources who make them look good.
- **Respond fast and honestly.** Journalists work on deadlines; a fast, straight answer (even "I can't comment
  on that, but here's what I can say") earns trust.

## Executive & thought-leadership comms

- **A point of view, not a promo.** Thought leadership earns authority by saying something genuinely useful or
  contrarian about the *industry*, not by pitching the product. The moment it becomes an ad, it loses authority.
- **Ground it in the exec's real experience and voice** — specific, opinionated, honest. Ghostwritten platitudes
  fool no one (the dev-marketing candor rules in `product-marketing.md` apply double here).
- **Channels:** owned (LinkedIn, a byline, a newsletter — see `social-media.md`), earned (contributed articles,
  podcasts, panels), and quotes in others' stories.
- **Consistency builds the asset.** A recognizable POV delivered regularly compounds into authority; one big
  op-ed does little alone.

## Crisis communications

When something goes wrong publicly, the response *is* the story. Principles:

- **Speed matters, but accuracy matters more.** Acknowledge fast that you're aware and looking into it; don't
  speculate or commit to facts you haven't verified. A silent vacuum gets filled by others.
- **Tell the truth, take responsibility.** Spin and minimization make it worse and last longer. Own what's
  yours, plainly. People forgive handled mistakes; they don't forgive cover-ups.
- **Lead with the people affected**, not the company's inconvenience. Empathy first, logistics second.
- **One source of truth, consistent across channels.** Designate a spokesperson; align the statement, social,
  support, and internal messaging so there's no contradiction.
- **Say what you're doing about it** — the concrete steps and the timeline. "We're sorry" without action rings
  hollow.
- **Internal first, or simultaneously.** Employees hearing it from the news is its own crisis.
- **Don't lie, don't delete, don't go dark.** Every one of those becomes the second, bigger story.

The honesty bar here is absolute: never draft a statement that denies something true, fabricates a cause, or
shifts blame dishonestly. The right help in a crisis is the *honest* response that holds up, not the one that
sounds best today.

## Measuring PR

PR metrics are softer than performance marketing's — be honest about that rather than inventing precision.

- **Output** (what you did): pitches sent, releases issued.
- **Coverage** (what you earned): placements, outlet quality/reach, message pull-through (did they tell the
  story you wanted?), share of voice vs. competitors, sentiment.
- **Outcome** (what it moved): referral traffic, branded search lift, leads/signups attributable to coverage,
  reputation over time.
- **Don't claim a sales number PR can't prove.** Attribution is genuinely fuzzy here; report what's real
  (coverage, reach, sentiment, correlated lifts) and flag the uncertainty rather than fabricating a clean ROI.
  Vanity "ad value equivalency" (AVE) is widely discredited — don't use it.

## Quality bar

Before sending a pitch, release, or statement, check:

1. Is it actually news by a real hook — or is it an owned-channel post in disguise?
2. Is the pitch personalized to *this* journalist and led with the news, not the brand?
3. Is every fact verifiable, every stat sourced, every quote genuinely approved? (No fabrication, ever.)
4. Does it serve the journalist's readers, not just the company?
5. For a crisis: is it honest, fast-but-accurate, people-first, and consistent across every channel?
6. Are the claimed results honest about PR's fuzzy attribution, with no invented ROI or discredited AVE?
